# Category M: Motivation & Reward

You are evaluating whether the game motivates the player effectively. Focus on the relationship between effort, achievement, and reward — whether the game's incentive structure drives engagement through genuine satisfaction rather than compulsive manipulation, and whether rewards feel earned, proportional, and meaningful.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### M1. Intrinsic Motivation Support (0-5)

Examine whether the game's core activities are rewarding in themselves. Check:
- Is mastering the game's systems satisfying independent of any score or reward the game grants?
- Does discovering how mechanics interact feel good on its own, or only because of an associated unlock or achievement?
- Does the game support self-expression and creative problem-solving as sources of satisfaction, or does it reduce everything to extrinsic metrics?

Score 0 if the game's activities are not enjoyable in themselves — the player endures the gameplay to receive rewards. Score 2 if some activities are intrinsically satisfying but others are pure grind that exists only to gate extrinsic rewards. Score 4 if the core activities — making decisions, exploring systems, solving problems — are genuinely rewarding in themselves, and any extrinsic rewards are secondary to the satisfaction of skilled play.

### M2. Extrinsic Motivation Appropriateness (0-5)

Identify all extrinsic reward systems (scores, unlocks, level-ups, achievements, currency). For each, check:
- Does the reward enhance an already enjoyable activity, or does it replace the enjoyment?
- Would the player still want to perform the activity without the extrinsic reward, or has the reward become the only reason to play?
- Do extrinsic rewards direct attention toward interesting parts of the game, or do they create busywork the player wouldn't otherwise do?

Score 0 if extrinsic rewards have cannibalized intrinsic motivation — the player plays for the numbers, not the experience, and would stop immediately if rewards were removed. Score 2 if extrinsic rewards are present and mostly additive, but some reward structures create perverse incentives (grinding, ignoring interesting content to optimize metrics). Score 4 if extrinsic rewards complement intrinsic motivation — they celebrate achievements the player already finds satisfying and direct attention toward the game's most engaging content.

### M3. Reward-Effort Correlation (0-5)

Compare the effort required by different activities to the rewards they yield. Check:
- Do harder challenges produce proportionally greater rewards than easier ones?
- Are there activities where the effort-to-reward ratio is clearly broken — high effort for negligible reward, or trivial actions for outsized payoff?
- Does the correlation hold consistently, or are there systematic exceptions where the game rewards the wrong behavior?

Score 0 if reward is disconnected from effort — trivial actions yield large rewards while difficult challenges yield nothing, or all actions yield the same reward regardless of difficulty. Score 2 if the correlation mostly holds but there are notable exceptions where effort is poorly compensated or trivial actions are disproportionately rewarded. Score 4 if greater challenges consistently yield greater rewards — the player is always incentivized to pursue harder, more interesting problems because the payoff scales with the investment.

### M4. Reward Type Variety (0-5)

Catalog every type of reward the game offers. Check:
- Does the game reward the player with multiple types of payoff — increased power, new knowledge, access to new areas or mechanics, narrative consequences, aesthetic satisfaction?
- Or does every reward reduce to the same currency — experience points, gold, score?
- Do different types of challenges yield different types of rewards, or is the reward structure monotonic?

Score 0 if every reward is the same type — all progress is measured in a single dimension ("number go up"). Score 2 if the game offers two or three reward types but one clearly dominates and the others are vestigial. Score 4 if the game provides genuinely diverse rewards — power, knowledge, access, narrative consequence, and aesthetic satisfaction — with different activities yielding different types of payoff.

### M5. Reward Predictability Balance (0-5)

Examine the predictability of the game's reward structure. Check:
- Are some rewards reliably tied to specific actions — giving the player clear goals to pursue?
- Are other rewards surprising — appearing unexpectedly through discovery, exploration, or emergent events?
- Is the balance between predictable and surprising rewards well-calibrated, or does the game lean too far in either direction?

Score 0 if all rewards are either completely predictable (no excitement of discovery) or completely random (no sense of working toward goals). Score 2 if the game leans heavily toward one end — mostly predictable with rare surprises, or mostly random with little goal-directed pursuit. Score 4 if the game balances predictable rewards that drive goal pursuit with surprising rewards that create excitement — the player can work toward known objectives while still encountering unexpected payoffs.

### M6. Achievement Measurement (0-5)

Identify how the game communicates the player's success. Check:
- Can the player tell how well they are performing — are there concrete metrics, milestones, or comparisons?
- Is progress visible and measurable, or does the player have no way to evaluate their own performance?
- Do the measurements capture what actually matters (strategic quality, creative solutions) or just surface activity (actions taken, time spent)?

Score 0 if the player has no way to evaluate their own performance — no score, no feedback, no visible progress, and no basis for comparing one play session to another. Score 2 if some measurement exists but it is either too coarse to be useful or measures the wrong things (time played rather than quality of decisions). Score 4 if the game provides clear, meaningful measurements of success that reflect the quality of the player's decisions — the player can concretely assess their performance and track improvement.

### M7. Judgment Fairness (0-5)

Examine the game's evaluation of player performance. Check:
- Does the game's scoring, feedback, or outcome determination feel fair — does the player accept the game's judgment?
- Are negative outcomes traceable to player decisions rather than hidden mechanics, luck, or arbitrary rules?
- Does the game explain why the player succeeded or failed, or does it issue verdicts without justification?

Score 0 if the game's judgments feel arbitrary — the player cannot understand why they received a particular score, outcome, or penalty, and results seem disconnected from decisions. Score 2 if judgments are mostly fair but occasional outcomes feel unjust — the player sometimes cannot identify what they did wrong or right. Score 4 if the game's judgments are consistently perceived as fair — every outcome is traceable to player decisions, feedback is clear, and the player accepts results because they understand them.

### M8. Reward-to-Punishment Ratio (0-5)

Compare the magnitude of rewards for success to the magnitude of punishments for failure across the game's systems. Check:
- Does success feel more impactful than failure — does winning feel better than losing feels bad?
- When the player fails, is the penalty proportionate, or does it feel disproportionately harsh relative to the rewards for success?
- Does the overall balance encourage continued engagement — does the player feel motivated to try again after failure?

Score 0 if punishment dominates — failure is harshly penalized while success is barely acknowledged, creating a fear-driven experience where the player avoids risk. Score 2 if the ratio is roughly even — success and failure feel symmetrical, which can make the experience feel grinding or neutral. Score 4 if success is rewarded more impactfully than failure is punished — winning feels great, losing stings but doesn't devastate, and the overall balance encourages the player to take risks and keep playing.

### M9. Reward Without Achievement Absence (0-5)

Examine whether the game's rewards are earned through meaningful effort. Check:
- Are there rewards granted for trivial actions — participation trophies, automatic level-ups, rewards for simply being present?
- Does the player have to demonstrate skill, make good decisions, or overcome challenges to receive rewards?
- Do rewards feel earned when received, or do they feel hollow because the player didn't do anything noteworthy to get them?

Score 0 if the game showers the player with rewards for trivial or no effort — every action is praised, progress is automatic, and rewards feel meaningless because they are not earned. Score 2 if most rewards require some effort but there are notable instances of unearned rewards that dilute the value of genuine achievements. Score 4 if every reward is clearly earned through meaningful effort — the player receives recognition only for genuine accomplishment, making each reward feel deserved and valuable.
