# Category F: Uncertainty & Randomness

You are evaluating how the game creates, manages, and leverages uncertainty. Focus exclusively on the sources of uncertainty — randomness, hidden information, analytic complexity, and unpredictable agents — and whether they serve the game's design goals.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### F1. Performative Uncertainty (0-5)

Identify whether the game tests the player's ability to execute their intentions. Check:
- Is there a gap between knowing what to do and being able to do it? (E.g., timing, positioning, resource juggling under pressure.)
- Does the game require skill in execution, or is executing a plan trivial once conceived?
- Are there moments where the player's plan is sound but execution is the challenge?

Score 0 if there is no execution challenge — once the player decides what to do, doing it is trivially easy with no skill component. Score 2 if some actions require execution skill but the challenge is inconsistent or shallow (e.g., occasional time pressure but mostly untimed). Score 4 if execution is a consistent, meaningful challenge — the player must skillfully carry out plans, and the gap between "knowing what to do" and "doing it" is a reliable source of tension.

### F2. Solver's Uncertainty (0-5)

Examine the problems the game presents to the player. Check:
- Are there puzzles, dilemmas, or strategic challenges where the correct approach is genuinely non-obvious?
- Must the player think, analyze, or reason to find solutions, or are answers immediately apparent?
- Does the game present situations where multiple plausible approaches exist and the player must evaluate which is best?

Score 0 if every problem has an obvious solution — the player never needs to think about what to do because the right action is always transparent. Score 2 if problems sometimes require thought but many have obvious solutions or the challenge is shallow (e.g., one clearly best option with a few traps). Score 4 if the game consistently presents problems where the player must genuinely reason about their approach — solutions are non-obvious, multiple viable paths exist, and analysis is rewarded.

### F3. Player/AI Unpredictability (0-5)

Examine the behavior of non-player agents (AI enemies, NPCs, factions). Check:
- Do AI agents behave in ways the player cannot fully predict?
- Must the player model what the AI is "thinking" or likely to do?
- Do AI agents respond to the player's actions in ways that create new uncertainty?
- Or are AI behaviors trivially predictable (always attack, always patrol the same route)?

Score 0 if all AI agents behave in completely predictable patterns — the player can perfectly anticipate every AI action after observing it once. Score 2 if AI agents have some variety in behavior but are largely predictable once the player has seen their repertoire. Score 4 if AI agents create genuine uncertainty — the player must reason about AI intentions, can be surprised by AI decisions, and must adapt to unpredictable opponent behavior.

### F4. Randomness as Situation Generator (0-5)

Identify every use of randomness in the game. For each instance, classify it:
- Does the randomness create an interesting situation the player must respond to? (Input randomness: the dice create the problem.)
- Or does the randomness arbitrarily determine the outcome of a player decision? (Output randomness: the player acts, then the dice decide if it worked.)

Check whether random outcomes generate decisions or replace them.

Score 0 if randomness is used exclusively as output randomness — the player makes a choice and then a dice roll determines success with no further player input. Score 2 if the game mixes input and output randomness, with some random events creating situations but others arbitrarily resolving player choices. Score 4 if randomness primarily generates situations — random events create new problems, terrain, encounters, or constraints that the player must respond to with skill and judgment.

### F5. Analytic Complexity (0-5)

Examine the decision space at critical moments. Check:
- Can the player fully calculate the optimal move through exhaustive analysis, or is the decision space too large for perfect calculation?
- Must the player use judgment, intuition, or heuristic reasoning because exact optimization is intractable?
- Is the combinatorial space rich enough to resist "solving"?

Score 0 if the decision space is trivially small — the player can easily calculate the optimal move every turn with no need for judgment. Score 2 if some decisions have meaningful depth but many are calculable, or the complexity fluctuates between trivial and interesting. Score 4 if the decision space is consistently rich enough that the player must rely on strategic judgment rather than calculation — the game resists optimization and rewards intuition built through experience.

### F6. Perception Uncertainty (0-5)

Check whether the game creates uncertainty about the player's own perception of the game state. Look for:
- Fog of war, hidden information, deception, unreliable narration, ambiguous environmental cues.
- Situations where the player must decide whether their understanding of the current state is correct.
- Mechanics that make the player question "Am I seeing this right?" or "Is this what I think it is?"

Score 0 if the player always has perfect, reliable knowledge of the game state — there is no uncertainty about what is actually happening. Score 2 if some information is uncertain but the player's perception of the core game state is generally reliable. Score 4 if the game deliberately and effectively plays with the player's perception — hidden information, deception, and ambiguity create genuine uncertainty about the true state of affairs.

### F7. Uncertainty Calibration (0-5)

Assess the overall level of uncertainty across the game. Check:
- Is total uncertainty high enough to prevent the game from feeling predetermined?
- Is it low enough that the player feels their decisions matter?
- Does the player feel uncertain about outcomes without feeling helpless?
- Is there a sense that more information and better play reduce uncertainty, even if they don't eliminate it?

Score 0 if uncertainty is miscalibrated — either so low that outcomes are predictable and the game feels solved, or so high that outcomes feel arbitrary and player decisions feel irrelevant. Score 2 if uncertainty is roughly appropriate but has notable imbalances (e.g., some phases feel predetermined while others feel random). Score 4 if uncertainty is well-calibrated throughout — the player consistently feels that outcomes are uncertain enough to be exciting but that their decisions meaningfully influence those outcomes.

### F8. Uncertainty Source Diversity (0-5)

List every source of uncertainty in the game (randomness, hidden information, analytic complexity, agent unpredictability, execution challenge, perception ambiguity). Check:
- Does the game draw from multiple types of uncertainty?
- Or does it rely on a single source (e.g., all uncertainty comes from dice rolls)?
- Do different uncertainty sources interact to create richer uncertainty than any single source could?

Score 0 if the game has only one source of uncertainty (e.g., everything is a random number check with no analytic depth, hidden information, or unpredictable agents). Score 2 if two or three sources are present but one dominates heavily and the others are minor. Score 4 if the game draws from four or more uncertainty sources that interact meaningfully — randomness creates situations, analytic complexity prevents solving them, hidden information adds tension, and unpredictable agents force adaptation.

### F9. Outcome Uncertainty (0-5)

Play or simulate a full game session. Check:
- Is the final outcome genuinely uncertain until late in the session?
- Or can the player (or an observer) predict the result early on?
- Are there comeback mechanisms that prevent early advantages from being decisive?
- Is there dramatic tension about whether the player will succeed or fail?

Score 0 if the outcome is predictable early — either the player wins easily once they establish an advantage, or failure is inevitable early with no recovery path. Score 2 if the outcome is uncertain for a while but resolves predictably before the end (e.g., a clear winner emerges at the midpoint). Score 4 if the outcome is genuinely uncertain until the final phase — the game maintains tension about success or failure throughout, with realistic paths to both outcomes remaining open.

### F10. Skill vs. Chance Balance (0-5)

Across a full play session, estimate the proportion of outcomes determined by player skill versus chance. Check:
- Could a skilled player consistently beat a less skilled player, or is the game a coin flip regardless of skill?
- Does the balance between skill and chance match the game's apparent design intent?
- For a strategy game: does skill dominate? For a gambling game: does chance dominate appropriately?

Score 0 if the skill-chance balance is wrong for the game's intent — a strategy game dominated by luck, or a gambling game where skill eliminates all chance. Score 2 if the balance is roughly appropriate but some outcomes feel too luck-dependent or too deterministic for the game's goals. Score 4 if the balance precisely matches the game's intent — skilled players are reliably rewarded to the degree appropriate for the genre, and chance contributes exactly the amount of variability the design calls for.

### F11. Agency-Randomness Balance (0-5)

Examine the player's subjective experience of control. Check:
- After a loss, does the player feel they could have won with better play, or that luck was against them?
- After a win, does the player feel they earned it, or that they got lucky?
- Over multiple sessions, does skill clearly determine long-run outcomes even if individual sessions have variance?
- Does the player feel like the driver of their fate, or a passenger on a random ride?

Score 0 if the player has no sense of control — outcomes feel entirely determined by randomness and player decisions feel like they don't matter. Score 2 if the player feels some control but specific moments feel unfairly random (e.g., a critical hit decides the final battle, invalidating all prior strategy). Score 4 if the player consistently feels that their choices are the primary determinant of outcomes — randomness adds variety and challenge but never overrides good play.

### F12. Pre-Action vs. Post-Action Randomness (0-5)

Examine when randomness resolves relative to the player's decisions. Check:
- Is randomness primarily front-loaded? (The player is dealt a random situation — procedural layout, random hand, environmental conditions — and must decide how to respond.)
- Or is randomness primarily back-loaded? (The player chooses an action and then randomness determines whether it succeeds — hit chance, dodge roll, random outcome.)
- Which type dominates the player's experience of uncertainty?

Score 0 if randomness is entirely back-loaded — the player chooses actions and then waits for the dice to decide if they worked, with no opportunity to adapt to random circumstances. Score 2 if both types are present but back-loaded randomness dominates the player's experience of uncertainty. Score 4 if randomness is primarily front-loaded — the player faces random situations and must decide how to handle them, with the outcome primarily determined by their response rather than by further randomness.
