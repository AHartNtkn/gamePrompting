# Category R: Variety & Replayability

You are evaluating whether the game offers meaningful variety across and within playthroughs. Focus on whether different runs feel genuinely different — not just cosmetically reshuffled — and whether the game sustains interest over repeated play.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### R1. Procedural Generation Quality (0-5) `[CONDITIONAL: game uses procedural generation]`

If the game uses procedural generation, examine the outputs across multiple generated instances. Check:
- Do generated results differ structurally, or only in surface details (different numbers, same layout)?
- Does generation produce situations that require different strategies, or can the player use the same approach regardless of what was generated?
- Are there degenerate outputs — generated content that is trivially easy, impossibly hard, or nonsensical?

Score 0 if generation produces superficial variation only — different names on the same structure, random numbers that don't change the strategic situation. Score 2 if generated content sometimes produces meaningfully different situations but frequently falls into samey patterns or produces occasional degenerate results. Score 4 if each generated instance creates a recognizably different strategic situation that demands a different approach, with no degenerate outputs observed.

### R2. Playthrough Variation (0-5)

Play or simulate the game multiple times using different approaches. Check:
- Do different playthroughs produce different events, encounters, or situations?
- Does the game's arc unfold differently, or does it follow the same scripted sequence with minor variations?
- After completing two playthroughs, could you distinguish them by their gameplay narrative, or would they be interchangeable?

Score 0 if every playthrough follows the same sequence of events and produces the same experience regardless of player choices or randomization. Score 2 if playthroughs differ in some respects (different random events, different resource distributions) but the overall arc and strategic shape are the same. Score 4 if different playthroughs produce genuinely distinct gameplay narratives — different challenges arise, different strategies are viable, and the experience feels fresh.

### R3. Starting Condition Variety (0-5) `[CONDITIONAL: game has multiple starting conditions]`

If the game offers different starting positions, factions, characters, or configurations, compare them. Check:
- Do different starting conditions create genuinely different strategic problems, or are they cosmetically different paths to the same gameplay?
- Does each starting condition have its own strengths, weaknesses, and implied strategy?
- Would a player need to change their approach based on which starting condition they chose?

Score 0 if starting conditions are cosmetically different but strategically identical — different names and art, same optimal play. Score 2 if starting conditions create some initial differences that converge to the same gameplay after the first few turns. Score 4 if each starting condition fundamentally shapes the strategic landscape for the entire playthrough, requiring distinct approaches and producing distinct experiences.

### R4. Player-Driven Variety (0-5)

Assess whether variety comes from player decisions rather than from randomization. Check:
- Can two players facing identical game states make different strategic choices that lead to meaningfully different outcomes?
- Does the game support multiple viable strategies, or is there one dominant approach?
- Is the variety the player experiences primarily a result of their own choices, or primarily a result of the random number generator?

Score 0 if all variety comes from randomization and the player's choices don't meaningfully alter the experience — the game plays the player. Score 2 if some player choices matter but the major sources of variation are external (random events, procedural generation) rather than strategic. Score 4 if the primary source of variety across playthroughs is player decision-making — different strategic philosophies lead to fundamentally different experiences even when the game's random elements are similar.

### R5. Long-Term Replayability (0-5)

Consider the game's decision space and variety mechanisms in aggregate. Check:
- How many playthroughs would it take before the player has encountered every major situation the game can produce?
- Does the game reveal new strategic dimensions on repeated play, or is the first playthrough representative of all future ones?
- Are there strategies or approaches that only become apparent after multiple playthroughs?

Score 0 if a single playthrough exhausts the game's content and strategic possibilities — there is nothing new to discover or try on a second run. Score 2 if the game offers a few playthroughs worth of distinct content but the strategic space is largely explored within three to five runs. Score 4 if the game's combinatorial space, strategic depth, and variety mechanisms sustain genuinely fresh experiences across many playthroughs, with new discoveries and strategies continuing to emerge.

### R6. Phase Differentiation (0-5)

Trace a single playthrough from beginning to end. Check:
- Do different phases of the game (early, mid, late) present different challenges, priorities, and decision types?
- Does the player's strategic focus shift as the game progresses, or is every turn the same kind of decision?
- Are there distinct transitions between phases — moments where the game's character changes?

Score 0 if the game feels the same from beginning to end — the same decisions, the same priorities, the same rhythm throughout. Score 2 if there is some evolution over the course of a playthrough but it is gradual and doesn't fundamentally change what the player is doing. Score 4 if the game has clearly distinct phases that each feel like a different game within the same system — different resources matter, different strategies are viable, and the player's focus shifts meaningfully.
