# Category G: Economy & Resource Design `[CONDITIONAL: game has economy/resources]`

You are evaluating the design of the game's economy and resource systems. This entire category applies only if the game has resources the player manages (currency, items, health, mana, supplies, population, etc.). If the game has no economy or resource management, score every criterion N/A (0/0). Focus exclusively on whether resources create meaningful decisions and maintain their value throughout play.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### G1. Resource Management Centrality (0-5)

Identify every resource in the game (health, gold, ammo, food, time, action points, etc.). Check:
- Do resource allocation decisions form a meaningful part of gameplay, or are they peripheral?
- Does the player regularly face decisions about how to spend scarce resources?
- Are resource decisions connected to the game's core mechanic, or are they isolated bookkeeping?

Score 0 if resources exist but managing them is trivial or disconnected from gameplay — the player never faces a meaningful resource decision. Score 2 if resource management is present and occasionally matters but is secondary to other systems and rarely creates difficult choices. Score 4 if resource management is central to the experience — the player regularly faces consequential decisions about what to spend, what to save, and what to sacrifice, and these decisions are tightly connected to the game's core.

### G2. Faucet-Sink Balance (0-5)

Trace the flow of each resource through the game. Identify every source (faucet) and every drain (sink). Check:
- Is the rate of resource generation balanced by the rate of consumption?
- Does accumulation plateau, or can the player stockpile indefinitely?
- Is there a steady state where income roughly matches expenditure, or does one side dominate?
- Do resources cycle (earned, spent, earned again) or only accumulate?

Score 0 if faucets and sinks are badly mismatched — resources either pile up endlessly (making scarcity meaningless) or drain faster than they can be earned (making the game unwinnable). Score 2 if the balance is roughly correct but there are periods of clear imbalance (e.g., mid-game surplus that eliminates tension). Score 4 if faucets and sinks are well-tuned — resources flow at rates that maintain meaningful scarcity throughout, with income and expenditure in productive tension.

### G3. Inflation Resistance (0-5)

Compare the value of resources at the start of the game to their value later. Check:
- Does a unit of currency buy roughly equivalent value throughout the game, or does purchasing power collapse?
- Do late-game resource quantities dwarf early-game quantities, making early resource decisions retroactively meaningless?
- Are costs calibrated to scale with income, or does the player outgrow the economy?

Score 0 if the economy inflates out of control — resources that mattered early become meaningless because the player has orders of magnitude more than they need. Score 2 if inflation exists but is partially contained (e.g., new expensive options appear but basic costs don't scale). Score 4 if resources maintain their value throughout play — every unit of resource remains meaningful from start to finish because costs, incomes, and available options scale together.

### G4. Pinch Point Design (0-5)

Identify moments where resources become scarce enough to force genuine trade-offs. Check:
- Are there points where the player must choose between competing needs because they can't afford both?
- Are these pinch points designed (intentional scarcity) or accidental (broken economy)?
- Do pinch points occur at dramatically appropriate moments, or do they feel arbitrary?
- Is the game ever too generous, eliminating the need for trade-offs?

Score 0 if the player never faces genuine resource scarcity — there is always enough for everything, so resource decisions have no weight. Score 2 if pinch points exist but are infrequent or poorly timed (e.g., scarcity at the start but abundance thereafter). Score 4 if the game consistently creates well-designed moments of scarcity — the player regularly faces genuine "guns or butter" decisions where resource allocation has real consequences.

### G5. Currency Differentiation (0-5)

List every resource type in the game. For each pair, check:
- Do they serve genuinely different purposes?
- Or are they functionally interchangeable (e.g., two types of "attack currency" that both just deal damage)?
- Could any two resources be collapsed into one without losing strategic depth?
- Does each resource create its own unique set of trade-offs?

Score 0 if multiple resources exist but are functionally identical — different names for the same thing, creating no additional strategic depth. Score 2 if resources are somewhat differentiated but some overlap exists (e.g., two resources that both primarily buy combat upgrades). Score 4 if every resource serves a clearly distinct purpose — each creates unique trade-offs that no other resource creates, and the interaction between different resource types is itself a source of strategy.

### G6. Resource Trade-off Clarity (0-5)

When the player faces a resource allocation decision, check:
- Can the player reason about the opportunity cost? (If I spend X on A, what am I giving up by not spending it on B?)
- Are the outcomes of resource spending predictable enough to allow meaningful comparison?
- Can the player evaluate whether a purchase or investment was "worth it" after the fact?
- Or is spending resources a blind gamble because the player can't estimate the value of alternatives?

Score 0 if opportunity costs are invisible — the player has no way to compare the value of different resource uses and must spend blindly. Score 2 if the player can roughly estimate costs and benefits for some resource decisions but others are opaque. Score 4 if the player can clearly reason about every resource trade-off — the costs, benefits, and alternatives are transparent enough for informed decision-making, even if the optimal choice is not obvious.

### G7. Endogenous Value (0-5)

Examine why resources matter within the game's internal logic. Check:
- Do resources have value because the game's systems make them genuinely useful?
- Does the player care about earning and spending resources because of what they enable within the game?
- Or do resources feel like arbitrary score counters with no meaningful connection to gameplay?
- Is there a functional reason to want more of each resource (it unlocks options, prevents consequences, enables strategies)?

Score 0 if resources have no meaningful connection to gameplay — they are numbers that go up with no functional impact on the player's situation. Score 2 if resources are somewhat useful but their value feels abstract (e.g., gold exists and can buy things, but the things it buys don't meaningfully change the player's strategy). Score 4 if every resource matters because it directly enables, constrains, or transforms gameplay — the player viscerally cares about resources because they are the fuel for meaningful action.

### G8. Economic Dynamism (0-5)

Examine how the economy changes over the course of a play session. Check:
- Do prices, availability, scarcity levels, or resource types change based on player actions or world events?
- Does the economic landscape shift, forcing the player to adapt their resource strategy?
- Or is the economy static — the same items at the same prices with the same resource flows from start to finish?

Score 0 if the economy is completely static — nothing about resource availability, costs, or flows changes over the course of the game. Score 2 if some economic changes occur (e.g., new items become available at fixed intervals) but the player doesn't need to adapt their resource strategy. Score 4 if the economy is genuinely dynamic — resource availability, costs, and trade-offs shift in response to player actions and world events, requiring the player to continuously reevaluate their resource strategy.
