# Category L: Player Agency & Freedom

You are evaluating whether the player feels in control of their experience. Focus on whether the game's structure empowers player-driven decisions, supports self-directed goals, and respects the player's ability to shape outcomes — not whether the game is permissive, but whether the player's choices genuinely matter and the game treats them as the author of their experience.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### L1. Autonomy (0-5)

Examine the game's decision structure and identify who is driving the experience. Check:
- Does the player choose what to do and when to do it, or does the game dictate a sequence of required actions?
- Are the player's choices self-directed — motivated by their own goals and reasoning — or are they responses to the game's demands?
- Does the game present options and let the player decide, or does it funnel the player through a predetermined path with the illusion of choice?

Score 0 if the player is driven entirely by the game — every action is either forced or the "choice" has only one viable option. Score 2 if the player has some freedom in how they approach tasks but the tasks themselves are dictated by the game. Score 4 if the player genuinely directs their own experience — they choose what goals to pursue, what strategies to employ, and what to prioritize, with the game providing the world rather than the agenda.

### L2. Freedom (0-5)

Assess the constraints the game places on the player. Check:
- Can the player pursue their own goals within the game's systems, or must they follow the game's prescribed objectives?
- Are there artificial barriers that exist for pacing rather than simulation reasons — locked areas, arbitrary prerequisites, gated content?
- Does the game's structure feel like a world to explore or a corridor to walk through?

Score 0 if the game is a rigid sequence of mandatory steps with no room for player-directed exploration or goal-setting. Score 2 if the player has some freedom within individual encounters or levels but the overall trajectory is fixed. Score 4 if the player feels genuinely free — they can set their own goals, explore at will, and engage with the game's systems on their own terms without artificial restrictions.

### L3. Player-as-Star (0-5)

Examine whether the game centers the player's experience. Check:
- Is the game about what the player does, or does the player watch things happen?
- Are significant events in the game caused by the player's decisions, or do they occur independently?
- Does the game frame the player as the protagonist of the experience — the person whose choices drive outcomes — or as a participant in the game's predetermined story?

Score 0 if the player is peripheral to what happens — events unfold regardless of player input and the game's systems operate autonomously. Score 2 if the player influences some outcomes but major events are predetermined or driven by the game's internal logic without player input. Score 4 if the player is clearly the center of the experience — every significant event is either caused by or meaningfully shaped by the player's decisions.

### L4. Player Authorship (0-5)

Trace the causal chain from player decisions to outcomes. Check:
- Can the player look at the current game state and identify it as the result of their specific decisions?
- Would different decisions have produced a meaningfully different state?
- Does the player feel like they authored what happened, or that they witnessed what the game produced?

Score 0 if the game state would be roughly the same regardless of what the player chose — decisions don't have lasting consequences. Score 2 if some decisions leave visible marks on the game state but the overall arc is similar across playthroughs. Score 4 if the game state is deeply shaped by player decisions — the current situation is a direct, traceable consequence of the player's specific choices, and different decisions would have produced a recognizably different outcome.

### L5. Recovery from Errors (0-5)

Make deliberate suboptimal choices during simulated play and observe the consequences. Check:
- Can the player recover from a bad decision through subsequent good play, or does one mistake cascade into irreversible failure?
- Does the game treat errors as part of play — opportunities to adapt and learn — or as punishments?
- Is there a reasonable window between "mistake made" and "game over," giving the player time to respond?

Score 0 if a single mistake can end the game or create an unrecoverable state with no warning. Score 2 if recovery is possible from some errors but certain mistakes are silently catastrophic — the player doesn't know they've lost until much later. Score 4 if the game treats errors as part of play — mistakes have consequences but the player can adapt, recover, and learn from them without the game ending abruptly or entering an unwinnable state.

### L6. Token Identification (0-5)

Examine the player's in-game representation and check:
- Does the player have a clear game token, avatar, faction, or position that represents them in the game world?
- Can the player develop attachment to or identification with their in-game representation?
- Does the game token accumulate history — does it reflect what the player has done and become?

Score 0 if the player has no identifiable in-game representation, or the representation is generic and interchangeable with no accumulated identity. Score 2 if the player has a game token but it is largely static — it doesn't reflect the player's decisions or develop over time. Score 4 if the player's in-game representation is distinct, accumulates meaningful history from the player's decisions, and becomes something the player identifies with and feels ownership over.

### L7. Expression (0-5)

Examine whether the game supports different styles of play that reflect player personality. Check:
- Can the player make choices that reflect their preferences, values, or personality rather than pure optimization?
- Does the game support multiple viable approaches that differ in character, not just efficiency?
- Can the player craft an identity through play — a style that is recognizably "theirs"?

Score 0 if there is one correct way to play and any deviation is strictly suboptimal — the game demands optimization, not expression. Score 2 if some room for personal expression exists but the viable strategy space is narrow enough that most players converge on similar approaches. Score 4 if the game actively supports diverse play styles — players can make choices based on personal preference and still succeed, creating play experiences that reflect their individual character.

### L8. Multiple Solution Paths (0-5)

Identify the game's core challenges and test whether each can be approached differently. Check:
- Can the player solve problems using different mechanics, strategies, or combinations of tools?
- Are alternate approaches genuinely different (different mechanics, different trade-offs) or just cosmetic variations?
- Do different solution paths produce different consequences, further differentiating the experience?

Score 0 if every problem has exactly one solution — the player must find the intended answer. Score 2 if some problems allow alternate approaches but most have a clearly dominant solution that makes alternatives pointless. Score 4 if problems consistently support multiple genuinely different approaches — different mechanics, different trade-offs, different consequences — and no single approach dominates.

### L9. Autonomy Respect (0-5)

Examine whether the game respects the player's ability to direct themselves. Check:
- Does the game avoid forcing the player down a single path through artificial constraints, mandatory tutorials, or unskippable sequences?
- When the game presents choices, are the branches meaningfully different or do they converge to the same outcome?
- Does the game trust the player to find their own way, or does it constantly redirect them toward the "correct" experience?

Score 0 if the game constantly overrides player choice — mandatory paths, false choices that converge, forced sequences that ignore player preference. Score 2 if the game provides some genuine choices but periodically forces the player onto a fixed path or invalidates earlier decisions. Score 4 if the game consistently respects player decisions — choices lead to genuinely different outcomes, the player's chosen path is honored, and the game does not redirect them toward a predetermined experience.

### L10. Mechanical Identity Expression (0-5)

Examine whether the game's systems support structurally different ways of playing. Check:
- Can the player develop a playstyle that is mechanically distinct — engaging with different systems, pursuing different strategies, facing different challenges — not just cosmetically different?
- Can two experienced players have fundamentally different experiences based on how they choose to engage with the game's mechanics (e.g., combat focus vs. trade focus vs. diplomacy focus)?
- Does the game's system breadth support these divergent approaches, or does every player eventually converge on the same mechanical experience?

Score 0 if all players engage with the same mechanics in the same way — there is one mechanical experience regardless of player preference. Score 2 if some mechanical variation exists but the core loop is the same for everyone — differences are at the margins rather than defining the experience. Score 4 if the game supports fundamentally different mechanical identities — players can choose structurally distinct ways of engaging with the systems, producing genuinely different gameplay experiences within the same game.
