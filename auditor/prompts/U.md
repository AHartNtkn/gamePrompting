# Category U: Specific Design Lenses

You are evaluating additional design dimensions drawn from specific frameworks that cut across the other categories. These criteria address emotional resonance, conflict structure, pleasure diversity, systemic rhetoric, and common anti-patterns. Evaluate each independently.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### U1. Emotion Alignment (0-5)

Identify what emotions the game appears to intend (from its theme, framing, aesthetic, and mechanics). Then check:
- Does actual gameplay evoke those emotions? Does a horror game feel tense? Does a power-fantasy game feel empowering?
- Are there moments where the emotional tone breaks — where the mechanics produce feelings at odds with the intended experience?
- Is the emotional palette consistent, or does the game accidentally oscillate between incompatible emotional states?

Score 0 if the game's mechanics produce emotions completely unrelated to its apparent intent — a game framed as tense survival that plays as relaxed resource management, or a comedy game with frustrating mechanics. Score 2 if the intended emotions appear intermittently but are frequently undermined by mechanics that produce the wrong feeling. Score 4 if gameplay consistently evokes the emotions the game is designed around — the player feels what the game wants them to feel throughout.

### U2. Essential Experience (0-5)

Identify the core experience the game is trying to create — the one thing it wants the player to feel or do above all else. Then check:
- Does every element in the game serve this essential experience?
- Are there systems, mechanics, or features that distract from or dilute the core experience?
- Could you remove any element without weakening the essential experience?

Score 0 if no coherent essential experience is identifiable — the game is a collection of features with no unifying experiential purpose. Score 2 if an essential experience is discernible but several elements work against it or pull in unrelated directions. Score 4 if every element in the game visibly serves a single, coherent essential experience — nothing distracts, nothing dilutes, and the game is ruthlessly focused on delivering one thing well.

### U3. Conflict Structure (0-5)

Identify what the player is struggling against. Check:
- Is the source of conflict clear — does the player know what stands between them and their goals?
- Is the conflict engaging — does it create tension, require effort, and produce satisfying resolution?
- Is the conflict well-matched to the mechanics — do the game's systems give the player appropriate tools to engage with the conflict?

Score 0 if there is no identifiable conflict or the conflict is so vague that the player doesn't know what they're struggling against. Score 2 if a conflict exists but it is either too abstract to engage with emotionally or poorly served by the game's mechanics. Score 4 if the conflict is clear, engaging, and perfectly matched to the game's mechanical tools — the player knows what they're fighting, cares about the outcome, and has meaningful ways to engage.

### U4. Multiple Pleasure Types (0-5)

Catalog the types of pleasure the game produces. Check:
- Does the game offer more than one type of satisfaction? (Intellectual challenge, discovery, optimization, narrative curiosity, aesthetic appreciation, power fantasy, social dynamics, etc.)
- Do different pleasure types emerge at different points in the game or from different activities?
- Are the pleasure types complementary, or do they compete for the player's attention?

Score 0 if the game produces only a single type of pleasure (or none) — every moment of engagement feels the same. Score 2 if two or three pleasure types are present but one dominates so heavily that the others are negligible. Score 4 if the game produces multiple distinct pleasure types that complement each other and emerge naturally from different aspects of gameplay — the player's experience is multi-dimensional.

### U5. Cultural Rhetoric (0-5)

Examine what the game's systems implicitly argue about how the world works. Check:
- Do the mechanics encode assumptions or values? (E.g., a game where all problems are solved by violence argues that violence is the primary tool of agency.)
- Is the game aware of what its systems are saying, or does it accidentally endorse positions its designers might not intend?
- Does the systemic rhetoric align with the game's stated theme, or does it contradict it?

Score 0 if the game's systems make incoherent or accidentally offensive arguments — the mechanics say something the game clearly doesn't intend, or they encode contradictory values. Score 2 if the game's systemic rhetoric is present but unexamined — the mechanics make arguments the designers probably didn't think about, though they aren't harmful. Score 4 if the game demonstrates awareness of its systemic rhetoric — the mechanics intentionally encode values that align with the game's theme and produce a coherent statement through play.

### U6. Item Enhancement (0-5) `[CONDITIONAL: game has improvable items/elements]`

If the game allows items, units, or other elements to be improved through gameplay, check:
- Does the improvement system offer meaningful choices (what to improve, how to improve it) or is it purely linear (everything gets better in the same way)?
- Does improving elements change how they function, or just increase their numbers?
- Does the improvement system create interesting decisions about resource allocation?

Score 0 if improvement is purely numerical — items gain higher stats with no qualitative change, no choices, and no trade-offs. Score 2 if improvement involves some choices but the options are shallow or one path is clearly dominant. Score 4 if improvement fundamentally changes how elements function, involves genuine trade-offs, and creates a meaningful decision space about what to enhance and how.

### U7. Identification System Quality (0-5) `[CONDITIONAL: game has unidentified elements]`

If the game features elements that must be identified (unknown items, unexplored regions, hidden faction traits), check:
- Does the identification process create interesting gameplay — gathering clues, taking risks, making inferences?
- Does identification resist trivialization through external knowledge (wikis, memorization from prior playthroughs)?
- Is there meaningful cost or risk to identification, making it a real decision rather than a chore?

Score 0 if identification is trivial — either there is no real mystery (elements are easily looked up) or identification is purely mechanical with no decision-making involved. Score 2 if identification involves some gameplay but is easily trivialized by external knowledge or by repeating a simple action. Score 4 if identification is a genuine gameplay system with meaningful costs, risks, and rewards, and resists trivialization through procedural variation or contextual clues that differ between playthroughs.

### U8. Power Without Gameplay Absence (0-5)

Search for mechanics that provide benefits without player awareness or decision-making. Check:
- Are there passive buffs, automatic bonuses, or background systems that help the player without them knowing or choosing?
- Does every source of power in the game involve a decision — when to use it, where to apply it, what to trade off?
- Are there stats or bonuses that the player can't meaningfully interact with — they just happen?

Score 0 if significant power comes from systems the player never interacts with — passive bonuses stack up invisibly, automatic processes run without player input or awareness. Score 2 if most power sources involve decisions but a few passive systems provide meaningful benefits with no player interaction. Score 4 if every source of power in the game is tied to a player decision — nothing happens automatically that the player doesn't choose, time, or direct.

### U9. Anti-Fun Balance (0-5)

Identify mechanics that impose negative experiences on the player. Check:
- Are there mechanics whose primary effect is to make the game feel worse (long mandatory waits, unavoidable punishment, tedious maintenance tasks)?
- For each negative mechanic, does it serve a design purpose that justifies its cost? (E.g., resource scarcity creates tension, permadeath creates stakes.)
- Is the negative impact proportional to the design benefit, or does the unfun vastly outweigh the purpose?

Score 0 if the game contains mechanics whose negative impact on the player experience far exceeds any design benefit — pure unfun with no justifying purpose. Score 2 if negative mechanics exist and serve a purpose but their implementation is heavier-handed than necessary — the unfun is disproportionate to the design goal. Score 4 if every negative mechanic is precisely calibrated — each one creates exactly enough friction to serve its purpose (tension, stakes, trade-offs) without a gram of excess unfun.

### U10. Interaction Richness (0-5)

Catalog every distinct, meaningful way the player can interact with the game system. Check:
- How many qualitatively different actions (verbs) does the player have?
- Does each verb produce distinct outcomes, or are multiple verbs functionally identical?
- Does the verb set cover the range of interactions the game's theme and setting imply?

Score 0 if the player has only one or two meaningful actions — the game's interaction model is impoverished relative to its scope. Score 2 if the player has a reasonable set of actions but several are rarely useful or functionally overlap with others. Score 4 if the player has a rich set of distinct, meaningful verbs that cover the full range of interactions the game's scope implies, with each verb earning its place through unique utility.

### U11. Information Management as Gameplay (0-5)

Assess whether information is treated as a resource the player must actively manage. Check:
- Must the player gather information through gameplay actions (scouting, interrogation, experimentation)?
- Are there decisions about what information to pursue, when to act on incomplete information, and how much to invest in intelligence?
- Does the game create interesting asymmetries between what the player knows and what they need to know?

Score 0 if information is either fully provided (no gathering needed) or fully hidden (no gathering possible) — there is no information management gameplay. Score 2 if some information must be gathered but the process is mechanical (explore tile to reveal it) with no strategic decisions about what to learn or when to act. Score 4 if information is a first-class resource — the player makes strategic decisions about what to learn, invests scarce resources in intelligence gathering, and must act under genuine uncertainty with meaningful consequences for both over-investing and under-investing in information.

### U12. Genre Awareness (0-5)

Identify the game's genre and its conventions. Check:
- Does the game demonstrate understanding of what has worked in its genre — does it implement genre staples competently?
- Where the game deviates from genre conventions, is the deviation purposeful and justified, or does it feel like an oversight?
- Does the game innovate within its genre, or does it merely replicate what exists without adding anything?

Score 0 if the game appears unaware of its genre's conventions — it omits standard features without replacement and includes nothing that demonstrates familiarity with the design space. Score 2 if the game implements genre basics competently but neither innovates nor demonstrates awareness of why conventions exist — it follows the template without understanding. Score 4 if the game demonstrates deep genre awareness — it implements conventions where they serve the design, deliberately departs where it has a better idea, and the overall result shows a designer who understands the genre's history and is contributing to it.
