# Category H: Challenge & Difficulty

You are evaluating how well the game calibrates its challenge. Focus exclusively on the difficulty curve, the quality of opposition, the fairness of outcomes, and whether failure serves the player's experience rather than punishing it.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### H1. Challenge Appropriateness (0-5)

Identify the game's target experience and implied audience. Then play through or simulate early, mid, and late-game scenarios. For each, check:
- Can the player succeed on their first or second attempt at a new challenge, or do they hit walls that require outside knowledge?
- Is there ever a point where the game is so easy that no real decision-making occurs?
- Does the challenge level match the game's framing? (A game billing itself as "brutal" should be hard; a "relaxing" game should not kill the player constantly.)

Score 0 if the game is either trivially solvable with no thought or impossibly hard with no reasonable path to success. Score 2 if the challenge is in the right ballpark but has spikes or dead zones where difficulty is clearly miscalibrated. Score 4 if the challenge consistently asks the player to think and act at a level that feels demanding but achievable throughout the game.

### H2. Skill-Challenge Balance (0-5)

Trace how the game adjusts what it asks of the player relative to what the player has had opportunity to learn. Check:
- After learning a new mechanic, is the player immediately tested on it at an appropriate level, or are they thrust into situations that require mastery they haven't had time to develop?
- Does the game ever become boring because the player's skill has outpaced the challenge?
- Does the game ever become frustrating because the challenge has outpaced the player's opportunity to learn?

Score 0 if the game makes no attempt to match challenge to skill — early game is as hard as late game, or harder. Score 2 if the game roughly tracks skill but has notable zones of boredom or anxiety. Score 4 if the player consistently feels engaged at the edge of their ability — challenged but not overwhelmed, tested but not bored.

### H3. Challenge Variety (0-5)

Catalog the types of problems the game presents. Check:
- Does the game test different skills or require different approaches across its challenges?
- Or does every obstacle reduce to the same type of problem (e.g., every encounter is solved by the same tactic, every puzzle uses the same logic)?
- Do different game phases introduce different challenge types?

Score 0 if every challenge is functionally identical — the player solves the same problem repeatedly with no variation. Score 2 if two or three distinct challenge types exist but one dominates heavily. Score 4 if the game presents varied challenges that test different skills, strategies, or knowledge domains throughout play.

### H4. Difficulty Escalation (0-5)

Compare early-game challenges to mid-game and late-game challenges. Check:
- Does difficulty increase as the player progresses?
- Is the rate of increase proportional to the player's expected skill growth?
- Are there sudden difficulty spikes or extended plateaus where nothing changes?
- Does the late game demand more of the player than the early game in identifiable ways (more complex decisions, faster execution, deeper planning)?

Score 0 if difficulty is flat — the first challenge is indistinguishable in difficulty from the last. Score 2 if difficulty generally increases but with noticeable spikes, plateaus, or reversals. Score 4 if difficulty rises in a smooth, readable curve that keeps the player growing throughout the entire experience.

### H5. Opposition Quality (0-5)

Identify what stands between the player and their goals. For each source of opposition, check:
- Does it create genuine resistance that requires the player to think, plan, or adapt?
- Or is opposition token — obstacles that exist but require no real effort to overcome?
- Does overcoming opposition feel earned, or does it feel like going through the motions?

Score 0 if opposition is purely cosmetic — enemies that don't threaten, obstacles that don't impede, timers that don't pressure. Score 2 if opposition provides resistance but is predictable and rarely forces the player to change their approach. Score 4 if opposition is genuine, varied, and requires the player to engage meaningfully — success feels like an accomplishment.

### H6. Fairness (0-5)

Examine every outcome the player can experience — wins, losses, setbacks, and deaths. For each, trace backward to determine causation:
- Was the outcome determined by the player's decisions, or by factors outside their knowledge or control?
- When randomness is involved, did the player understand the odds and choose to accept them?
- Are there situations where the player can do everything right and still lose to hidden information, unavoidable traps, or arbitrary events?

Score 0 if outcomes feel arbitrary — the player cannot reliably connect their decisions to results. Score 2 if most outcomes are fair but occasional losses feel undeserved or unpredictable. Score 4 if the player can always trace a failure to a specific mistake they made, and successes clearly result from good play.

### H7. Consequence Proportionality (0-5)

Catalog the penalties the game imposes for mistakes. For each penalty, compare it to the severity of the error that triggered it:
- Can a minor misclick or small tactical error wipe out hours of progress?
- Are major strategic blunders punished more harshly than minor oversights?
- Is there a graduated scale of consequences, or is it binary (fine vs. catastrophic)?

Score 0 if small errors produce catastrophic consequences — a single wrong choice can destroy an entire run with no warning. Score 2 if most consequences are proportional but a few penalties are disproportionately severe. Score 4 if consequences scale smoothly with the severity of the mistake — small errors cost a little, big errors cost a lot, and the player can always read the risk beforehand.

### H8. Failure Informativeness (0-5)

After every failure state, check what information the player receives:
- Does the game tell or show the player why they failed?
- Can the player identify what they should do differently next time?
- Or does failure feel opaque — the player lost but doesn't know what decision led to the loss?

Score 0 if failure is a black box — the player dies or loses with no indication of cause. Score 2 if the player can sometimes infer what went wrong but must guess in many cases. Score 4 if every failure clearly communicates what the player did wrong and what alternative approach might succeed, either through explicit feedback or through game state that makes the cause obvious.

### H9. Head vs. Hands Balance (0-5)

Classify each challenge in the game as primarily mental (strategic thinking, planning, pattern recognition, resource management) or primarily execution-based (timing, reflexes, dexterity). Then check:
- Is the ratio appropriate for the game's apparent goals?
- For CLI games: since the interface is text-based, almost all challenge should be mental. Is it? Or does the game artificially inject execution challenges (e.g., time pressure on typing)?
- Does the game ever test the wrong skill for the situation? (E.g., a puzzle that requires fast input rather than clever reasoning.)

Score 0 if the game tests skills that are mismatched with its design — a strategy game that depends on typing speed, or a text game that requires twitch reflexes. Score 2 if the balance is mostly right but a few challenges test the wrong skill. Score 4 if every challenge tests the skill type that is appropriate for the game's design and the specific situation.

### H10. Dynamic Difficulty (0-5)

Examine how (or whether) the game adjusts to player skill over time. Check:
- Does the game offer explicit difficulty settings, or implicit ones through player-chosen paths?
- Can skilled players seek harder challenges? Can struggling players find easier ones?
- Does the game's internal state naturally create difficulty variation (e.g., player choices affecting future challenge levels)?
- If difficulty is static, is the static level well-chosen for the game's audience?

Score 0 if the game has a single fixed difficulty with no adaptation or player agency over challenge level. Score 2 if some difficulty adjustment exists but it is coarse or limited (e.g., easy/medium/hard at game start with no further adaptation). Score 4 if the game continuously presents opportunities for the player to self-select challenge level through their choices, or if internal systems naturally calibrate difficulty to player behavior.

### H11. No Instant Death (0-5)

Search for any game state where a single action or event can end the player's run or cause irrecoverable loss without prior warning. Check:
- Are there instant-kill mechanics? If so, are they telegraphed in advance?
- Can the player encounter lethal situations before they have the knowledge or tools to avoid them?
- Does the game distinguish between "this will hurt" and "this will kill" clearly enough for the player to make informed risk decisions?

Score 0 if the game regularly kills the player with no warning — unavoidable traps, instant death from unknown mechanics, one-hit kills from new enemies. Score 2 if instant-death scenarios exist but are mostly telegraphed, with a few unfair exceptions. Score 4 if every lethal situation is clearly communicated in advance, giving the player the information needed to avoid it or the tools to survive it.

### H12. Failure Generativity (0-5)

When the player fails, examine what game state results. Check:
- Does failure produce a new, playable situation with its own decisions and possibilities? Or does it simply reset or end the game?
- Can the player lose a battle but adapt and continue with an altered strategy?
- Does a failed state create emergent gameplay — new problems to solve that wouldn't exist without the failure?
- Is "losing is fun" a real possibility, or is failure just a gate back to the last checkpoint?

Score 0 if failure always means game-over or full reset — the only response to failure is to start again from scratch. Score 2 if some failures create interesting situations but most still lead to restart or reload. Score 4 if failure regularly produces compelling new game states — lost resources create scarcity decisions, defeated units change strategic options, and the player's story continues through setbacks rather than ending at them.
