# Category K: Engagement & Compulsion

You are evaluating whether the game creates genuine desire to keep playing. Focus on the mechanisms that generate forward momentum, curiosity, and intrinsic engagement — not whether the game is "fun" in the abstract, but whether its design produces specific, identifiable pulls that sustain play.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### K1. One More Turn Compulsion (0-5)

Examine the game's turn/cycle structure and identify what the player is looking forward to at any given moment. Check:
- At the end of each action or turn, is there a visible next thing the player is anticipating?
- Are there processes in motion whose outcomes the player is waiting to see resolve?
- Does the game create overlapping timelines — short-term payoffs arriving while medium-term plans are still unfolding?

Score 0 if the game gives no reason to continue after any given action — nothing is in motion, nothing is pending, and there is no forward pull. Score 2 if the player occasionally has something to look forward to, but there are dead stretches where nothing is anticipated. Score 4 if at every decision point there is at least one unresolved thread pulling the player forward — something is always about to happen or about to pay off.

### K2. Curiosity Generation (0-5)

Identify what questions the game raises in the player's mind during play. Check:
- Does the game present unexplored possibilities — regions, mechanics, combinations — that the player would naturally want to investigate?
- Does observing one system interaction raise questions about other potential interactions?
- Are there visible unknowns the player can choose to pursue ("What does this do?" "What's over there?" "What happens if I combine these?")?

Score 0 if the game raises no questions — everything is immediately apparent or nothing invites investigation. Score 2 if the game raises some initial questions but they are either answered too quickly or too few to sustain interest. Score 4 if the game continuously generates new questions as old ones are answered, creating a self-sustaining cycle of curiosity.

### K3. Surprise Factor (0-5)

Play through or simulate multiple sessions and track moments of genuine surprise. Check:
- Do system interactions produce outcomes the player did not predict but can understand after the fact?
- Are surprises emergent (arising from rule interactions) rather than scripted (random events or pre-authored twists)?
- Do surprises feel fair — consistent with established rules — rather than arbitrary?

Score 0 if the game never surprises the player, or if all surprises are random events with no logical basis. Score 2 if surprises occur but are infrequent, or if they are mostly scripted rather than emergent. Score 4 if the game regularly produces genuinely unexpected outcomes that logically follow from established rules — surprises feel earned, not imposed.

### K4. Discovery Reward (0-5)

Experiment with the game's systems and note whether discovering how they work is satisfying. Check:
- Does understanding a mechanic give the player new capabilities or strategies they didn't have before?
- Is the process of figuring out system interactions itself rewarding, or merely necessary to avoid failure?
- Does the game have layers — surface mechanics that work simply, with deeper interactions that reward investigation?

Score 0 if there is nothing to discover, or if discovering how systems work provides no practical or emotional payoff. Score 2 if some discoveries are rewarding but the game's systems are largely transparent from the start, leaving little to uncover. Score 4 if the game richly rewards systematic experimentation — understanding how things work opens new strategies, produces "aha" moments, and makes the player feel clever.

### K5. Novelty Continuation (0-5)

Simulate extended play across multiple sessions or game stages. Check:
- Does the game continue to introduce new situations, combinations, or challenges as play progresses?
- At what point (if ever) does the player see everything the game has to offer?
- Are later stages as surprising and fresh as early ones, or does the game become repetitive?

Score 0 if the game exhausts its novelty within the first few minutes — every subsequent play is a repeat of what came before. Score 2 if the game introduces some new elements over time but eventually settles into a predictable pattern. Score 4 if the game's combinatorial or procedural systems generate genuinely novel situations well into extended play — the possibility space remains open.

### K6. Autotelic Quality (0-5)

Examine whether the moment-to-moment activity of playing is enjoyable independent of outcomes. Check:
- Is the act of making decisions in this game interesting in itself, or does the player endure decisions to reach rewards?
- Would a player enjoy this game even if there were no score, no win condition, and no progress markers?
- Does the core interaction loop feel like play or like work?

Score 0 if the activity itself is tedious and the player would only continue for external rewards or to see a number increase. Score 2 if the core activity is tolerable but not something the player would seek out for its own sake. Score 4 if the core decision-making is genuinely engaging — the player would enjoy the process of playing even without rewards, scores, or progression.

### K7. Absorption Potential (0-5)

Assess whether the game creates conditions for deep focus and immersion. Check:
- Does the game maintain a consistent pace and challenge level that prevents the player from becoming bored or overwhelmed?
- Are there distractions, interruptions, or interface frictions that break concentration?
- Does the game's information density match its decision rate — enough to think about without being overloaded?

Score 0 if the game constantly breaks concentration through mandatory interruptions, trivial tasks, or jarring pace changes. Score 2 if the player can achieve focus during some portions of play but is regularly pulled out by interface issues, dead time, or information overload. Score 4 if the game creates sustained conditions for deep engagement — the player can enter a state of full focus where the interface disappears and only the decisions remain.

### K8. Problem-Solving Interest (0-5)

Catalog the types of problems the game asks the player to solve. For each, check:
- Is the problem genuinely interesting to reason about, or is it trivially obvious?
- Does solving the problem require thought, analysis, or creativity — not just execution?
- Are the problems the kind that the game's target audience would enjoy wrestling with?

Score 0 if the game's problems are either trivial (obvious best answer) or opaque (no rational basis for choosing). Score 2 if some problems are interesting but many are routine or mechanical. Score 4 if the game consistently presents problems that are satisfying to think about — they require genuine reasoning, have non-obvious solutions, and reward careful analysis.

### K9. Competence Satisfaction (0-5)

Track whether successful play produces a feeling of skill and effectiveness. Check:
- When the player succeeds, is it clear that their decisions caused the success (not luck)?
- Does the game give feedback that makes the player feel capable and effective?
- Does improving at the game feel rewarding — does the player perceive their own growing competence?

Score 0 if the player never feels effective — outcomes seem disconnected from decisions, or the game provides no feedback on successful play. Score 2 if the player occasionally feels skilled but the connection between decisions and outcomes is inconsistent. Score 4 if successful play clearly results from good decisions, the game provides clear feedback on effective play, and improving skill produces a tangible sense of growing mastery.

### K10. Narrative Emergence (0-5) `[CONDITIONAL: game has enough systemic breadth for narrative emergence]`

Simulate extended play and identify any "story-worthy" events that arise from system interactions. Check:
- Do interacting systems produce sequences of events that form a coherent narrative arc (setup, escalation, resolution)?
- Would a player naturally describe what happened in narrative terms ("My settlement was thriving until the drought hit, which caused...")?
- Are these narratives unique to this particular playthrough, or would any player see roughly the same events?

Score 0 if the game produces no emergent narratives — events are disconnected, systems don't chain, and nothing story-worthy happens. Score 2 if occasional interesting sequences occur but they are short, isolated, or not meaningfully shaped by the player's decisions. Score 4 if the game regularly generates multi-step emergent narratives that are unique to each playthrough, shaped by player decisions, and naturally invite retelling.

### K11. Narrative Emergence Rate (0-5) `[CONDITIONAL: game has enough systemic breadth for narrative emergence]`

Track the frequency of story-worthy emergent events across simulated play. Check:
- How many turns or minutes pass between narrative-quality events?
- Are story-worthy moments frequent enough to be a defining feature of the experience?
- Are they spaced well enough to maintain impact, or do they blur together through oversaturation?

Score 0 if story-worthy events never occur or are so rare they might as well not exist. Score 2 if narrative events happen occasionally but with long stretches of uneventful play between them — the game has narrative potential but doesn't realize it consistently. Score 4 if story-worthy events occur at a rate that makes them a reliable feature of play — frequent enough to sustain engagement but spaced enough that each one registers as significant.

### K12. Discoverable Systemic Interactions (0-5)

Examine the game's systems for interactions that are functional but not explicitly documented. Check:
- Are there meaningful interactions between systems that the player can discover through experimentation?
- Is the space of undocumented-but-functional interactions large enough for ongoing discovery, or is it exhausted quickly?
- Do discovered interactions provide practical benefit — new strategies, efficiencies, or capabilities — rather than being mere curiosities?

Score 0 if all system interactions are either explicitly documented or non-existent — there is nothing to discover. Score 2 if a few discoverable interactions exist but the space is small and quickly exhausted. Score 4 if the game contains a rich space of undocumented systemic interactions that reward experimentation — the player can continue discovering useful combinations well into extended play.

### K13. Non-Prescriptive Problem Structure (0-5)

Examine how the game presents challenges to the player. Check:
- When the player faces a problem, does the game avoid signaling a "correct" solution method?
- Are there highlighted weak points, obvious patterns, quest markers, or other cues that tell the player how to solve the problem rather than letting them figure it out?
- Does the game present the problem space and let the player bring their own approach, or does it guide them toward a predetermined solution?

Score 0 if every problem comes with a clear signal of how to solve it — highlighted weak points, quest arrows, or mechanics that only work one way. Score 2 if some problems are open-ended but others telegraph their solutions through obvious cues or constrained mechanics. Score 4 if the game consistently presents problems as open challenges with no preferred solution method — the player must assess the situation and devise their own approach using whatever tools and knowledge they have.
