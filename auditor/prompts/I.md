# Category I: Learning & Mastery

You are evaluating how well the game rewards the player's investment in understanding its systems. Focus exclusively on pattern depth, skill expression, learning curves, and whether knowledge is a meaningful form of progression.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### I1. Pattern Richness (0-5)

Identify the learnable patterns in the game — recurring dynamics, exploitable regularities, strategic principles. Then check:
- Are patterns present at multiple levels of complexity (surface-level tactics, mid-level strategies, deep systemic interactions)?
- Can a player who has mastered the obvious patterns still discover deeper ones?
- Are patterns interconnected, so that understanding one reveals another?

Score 0 if the game contains no learnable patterns — outcomes appear random or the game is so simple that nothing needs to be learned. Score 2 if a few patterns exist but they are shallow and quickly exhausted. Score 4 if the game contains layered patterns at multiple depths, where mastering one layer reveals the existence of the next.

### I2. Learning Curve Continuity (0-5)

Play through or simulate an extended session. Track the rate at which the player encounters new things to learn. Check:
- Is there always a next thing to learn, or does the player hit a plateau where they've seen everything?
- Does the game introduce new mechanics, complications, or strategic dimensions over time?
- After the initial learning burst, does the game maintain a supply of new patterns to discover?

Score 0 if the player learns everything the game offers within the first few minutes and the rest is repetition. Score 2 if learning continues for a while but eventually plateaus, leaving the player doing the same thing with no new understanding to gain. Score 4 if the game continuously presents new things to learn throughout the full duration of play — the player never stops growing.

### I3. Mastery Depth (0-5)

Compare how a beginner and an expert would play the same game. Check:
- How large is the performance gap between novice and master play?
- Are there identifiable skill tiers — distinct levels of understanding that produce measurably different results?
- Can a player spend many hours improving and still see room to grow?

Score 0 if beginner and expert play are indistinguishable — the game has no skill ceiling or the ceiling is so low that a few minutes of play reaches it. Score 2 if some improvement is possible but mastery is shallow — a moderate player is nearly as effective as an expert. Score 4 if the gap between novice and expert is vast, with many identifiable levels of mastery and clear evidence that deeper understanding produces better outcomes.

### I4. Grokking Resistance (0-5)

Estimate how long a focused, intelligent player would take to fully internalize all of the game's patterns. Check:
- Can the game be "solved" — all patterns learned and all outcomes predicted — in a single session?
- Does the game have enough strategic depth that even experienced players encounter situations they haven't fully optimized?
- Are there combinatorial interactions between systems that resist complete internalization?

Score 0 if the game can be fully understood within minutes — every pattern is obvious and no residual depth exists. Score 2 if the game takes several sessions to fully internalize but is eventually exhausted. Score 4 if the game resists complete internalization for a long time — players continue to discover new patterns, refine strategies, and encounter novel situations after extensive play.

### I5. Pattern Visibility (0-5)

When the player learns something, check whether they can perceive that they learned it:
- Are the game's underlying patterns surfaced through gameplay, or are they hidden behind opaque mechanics?
- Can the player form and test hypotheses about how the game works?
- When a pattern is present, does the game give the player the information needed to detect it?

Score 0 if the game's dynamics are opaque — the player cannot determine how the game works through play, and improvement feels accidental. Score 2 if some patterns are visible but others require external resources or blind experimentation to detect. Score 4 if every pattern in the game is discoverable through attentive play — the player has enough information to form hypotheses and enough feedback to confirm or reject them.

### I6. Skill-Expression Space (0-5)

Once a player has learned the game's patterns, examine how they can apply that knowledge:
- Is there only one correct way to play, or can mastery be expressed through different styles and approaches?
- Can two expert players play differently and both be effective?
- Does the game reward creative application of knowledge, not just rote execution of the optimal strategy?

Score 0 if there is exactly one correct strategy and all expert play converges to the same sequence of moves. Score 2 if some variation is possible but one approach is clearly dominant. Score 4 if mastery can be expressed through meaningfully different playstyles, strategies, or approaches — expert players make genuinely different choices based on preference rather than ignorance.

### I7. Degenerate Pattern Absence (0-5)

Search for dominant strategies — approaches so powerful that once discovered, they make all other strategies obsolete. Check:
- Is there any single strategy, build, or pattern that trivializes the game once known?
- Can the player find a "win button" — a repeatable approach that works in all situations?
- If a dominant strategy exists, does the game have any mechanism to counteract or prevent it?

Score 0 if a single dominant strategy exists that renders all other play irrelevant — once found, the game is solved. Score 2 if near-dominant strategies exist but they don't completely trivialize the game, or they work in most but not all situations. Score 4 if no degenerate patterns exist — every strategy has situations where it excels and situations where it fails, forcing the player to adapt.

### I8. Skills Developed Through Play (0-5)

Examine how the player acquires the skills needed to succeed. Check:
- Does the game teach its mechanics through gameplay rather than through text dumps, tutorials, or instruction manuals?
- Does the player develop skills by doing, or by reading?
- Are early challenges structured so that the player naturally practices the skills they'll need later?

Score 0 if the game requires the player to read extensive documentation before they can play effectively, or if skills are not developed at all. Score 2 if the game teaches some skills through play but others require external instruction or trial-and-error with no guidance. Score 4 if the game's structure naturally develops all necessary skills through gameplay — the player learns by playing, and early play prepares them for late play.

### I9. Skilled Play Rewarded (0-5)

Compare outcomes for skilled play versus unskilled play. Check:
- Does a player who understands the game's systems achieve measurably better results?
- Is the difference between good and bad play visible in scores, survival time, resources acquired, or other metrics?
- Or does randomness, stat inflation, or other factors wash out the effect of skill?

Score 0 if skill has no measurable effect on outcomes — a knowledgeable player performs about the same as an ignorant one. Score 2 if skill matters but its effect is partially obscured by randomness or other factors. Score 4 if skilled play produces clearly superior outcomes and the player can see the connection between their knowledge and their results.

### I10. Skill Ceiling Visibility (0-5)

Check whether the player can perceive that higher levels of play exist:
- Does the game show the player that there are things they haven't mastered yet?
- Can the player see other outcomes, scores, or states that they haven't achieved?
- Does the game surface the gap between current play and optimal play?

Score 0 if the player has no way to know whether they are playing well or poorly relative to what's possible. Score 2 if the player can vaguely sense there is more to learn but the ceiling is not concretely visible. Score 4 if the game actively shows the player that higher mastery exists — through scoring systems, visible optimal paths, or encounters with challenges they can't yet handle.

### I11. Competence Growth Feedback (0-5)

Track how the game communicates improvement to the player over time. Check:
- Does the game tell the player they are getting better? (Not just "you won" but "you won faster/better/more efficiently than before.")
- Are there metrics, comparisons, or milestones that make growth visible?
- Can the player look back and see how far they've come?

Score 0 if the game provides no feedback on improvement — the player cannot tell whether they are getting better. Score 2 if improvement is partially visible through outcomes but the game does not explicitly surface growth. Score 4 if the game actively tracks and communicates player improvement — through scores, statistics, unlocked capabilities, or direct comparison to past performance.

### I12. Knowledge-Gated Progression (0-5)

Examine what drives progression in the game — is it the player's understanding, or their character's accumulated power? Check:
- Could a knowledgeable player on a fresh start outperform a naive player with many hours invested?
- Is progression primarily gated by "knowing what to do" or by "having done enough grinding"?
- Do character stats, unlocks, or time-gates matter more than player knowledge?

Score 0 if progression is entirely stat-based — a naive player with high stats will always outperform a knowledgeable player with low stats. Score 2 if knowledge and stats both matter but stats dominate in many situations. Score 4 if a knowledgeable player on a fresh start is dramatically more effective than a naive player with extensive playtime — understanding the game is the primary form of power.

### I13. Lateral Power Acquisition (0-5)

Examine how the player's capabilities change over time. Check:
- Does progression give the player more options (new tools, new approaches, new situations they can handle)?
- Or does progression give the player bigger numbers (more damage, more health, more resources)?
- Does late-game equipment make early-game equipment obsolete, or does early gear remain situationally relevant?
- Can the player approach old problems in new ways as they progress, or do they just do the same thing harder?

Score 0 if progression is purely vertical — bigger numbers replace smaller numbers, old equipment becomes worthless, and late-game play is mechanically identical to early-game play with inflated values. Score 2 if progression is mostly vertical but some new options emerge. Score 4 if progression primarily expands the player's option space — new capabilities open new strategies, early tools remain useful in specific contexts, and the player's repertoire grows rather than their power level.
