# Category J: Pacing & Rhythm

You are evaluating how well the game manages time and tempo. Focus exclusively on the flow of play — whether every moment is purposeful, whether the experience escalates and varies appropriately, and whether the game's structure creates satisfying rhythms.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### J1. Pacing Quality (0-5)

Play through or simulate a representative session from start to finish. Track the rhythm of play moment by moment. Check:
- Does every turn, action, or input move the experience forward in some way?
- Is there a natural momentum to play, or does the game feel sluggish, rushed, or erratic?
- Does the pace match the game's intended experience? (A tense survival game should feel urgent; a contemplative strategy game should allow reflection.)

Score 0 if the game's pacing is broken — long stretches of nothing happening, or frantic activity with no breathing room, or wildly inconsistent tempo with no design intent behind it. Score 2 if the pacing is adequate in most sections but has noticeable dead spots or rushes. Score 4 if the pacing feels deliberate and well-controlled throughout — every moment has purpose and the tempo matches the intended experience.

### J2. Dead Time Absence (0-5)

Identify every moment where the player is waiting without having a decision to make. Check:
- Are there periods where the game is processing, displaying, or cycling through content while the player has nothing to do?
- Does the player ever wait for the game to "catch up" — animations, AI turns, text output — with no decisions available?
- When the player isn't actively choosing, is there at least something meaningful to observe or think about?

Score 0 if the game has frequent dead time — the player regularly waits with nothing to do, think about, or observe. Score 2 if dead time is occasional and brief but still present. Score 4 if the player always has something to decide, observe, or consider — there are no moments where they are simply waiting for the game to proceed.

### J3. Decision Rhythm (0-5)

Map the cadence of decisions across a play session. Check:
- Is there a steady, readable beat to the decision-action-feedback loop?
- Does the time between decisions feel natural, or are there long gaps followed by bursts?
- Can the player fall into a productive rhythm, or does the game constantly break their flow?

Score 0 if decisions are erratic — long stretches with no choices, then sudden bursts of rapid-fire decisions with no pattern. Score 2 if a rhythm exists but is uneven, with occasional disruptions or awkward gaps. Score 4 if the game maintains a clear, satisfying cadence — decisions arrive at regular intervals, each followed by feedback, creating a natural loop the player can settle into.

### J4. Escalation (0-5)

Compare the early game to the mid and late game in terms of complexity, stakes, and interest. Check:
- Does the game become more complex, higher-stakes, and more interesting as play progresses?
- Or does it peak early and become routine?
- Are new elements introduced over time that change the strategic landscape?
- Does the late game present challenges and situations that couldn't exist in the early game?

Score 0 if the game is most interesting in the first few minutes and becomes progressively less engaging. Score 2 if the game escalates somewhat but flattens out or even declines in the second half. Score 4 if the game continuously escalates — each phase is more complex, more consequential, and more interesting than the last.

### J5. Front-Loading Resistance (0-5)

Track how the game's strategic depth changes over time. Check:
- Does the game maintain or deepen its decision space as play progresses?
- Or do early decisions matter most, with later play becoming rote execution of an established plan?
- Does the late game offer as many meaningful choices as the early game, or has the decision space collapsed?

Score 0 if all interesting decisions happen at the start and the rest of the game plays itself — the player's late-game actions are predetermined by early choices. Score 2 if depth is maintained through the mid-game but thins out toward the end. Score 4 if depth is sustained or increases throughout the entire game — late-game decisions are at least as rich and consequential as early-game ones.

### J6. Interest Curve (0-5)

Map the emotional engagement across a play session. Check:
- Does the experience have peaks and valleys that create dramatic structure?
- Are there moments of tension followed by release, buildup followed by payoff?
- Or is the engagement level flat — either uniformly exciting (exhausting) or uniformly dull?

Score 0 if engagement is flat — the game feels monotone throughout, with no moments of heightened tension or satisfying release. Score 2 if some variation exists but peaks and valleys are not well-timed or feel accidental rather than designed. Score 4 if the game creates a compelling interest curve with deliberate peaks of tension, valleys of reflection, and clear dramatic arc.

### J7. Arc Structure (0-5)

Examine whether the game has distinct phases. Check:
- Is there a recognizable opening, midgame, and endgame?
- Does each phase have a different character — different priorities, different strategies, different feel?
- Or does the game feel the same from minute one to the last turn?
- Are phase transitions signaled to the player?

Score 0 if the game has no discernible phases — the experience is undifferentiated from start to finish. Score 2 if phases exist but are weakly defined or hard to distinguish. Score 4 if the game has clearly distinct phases, each with its own strategic texture and feel, with readable transitions between them.

### J8. Session Structure (0-5)

Examine how the game handles play sessions. Check:
- Does the game create natural stopping points — moments where the player feels satisfied enough to stop?
- Or is the experience formless, making it hard to find a good time to quit?
- Can the player identify when they've completed a meaningful unit of play?
- Does the game respect the player's time by making sessions feel self-contained?

Score 0 if the game has no natural stopping points — the player either quits mid-action or plays indefinitely with no sense of completion. Score 2 if stopping points exist but are infrequent or unclear. Score 4 if the game creates clear, satisfying session boundaries — the player regularly reaches moments where they've accomplished something complete and can comfortably stop.

### J9. Repetition Variation (0-5)

Identify the activities the player performs most frequently. For each repeated activity, check:
- Does each repetition introduce enough variation to feel distinct from the last?
- Are there different approaches, different contexts, or different stakes that make repeated actions feel fresh?
- Or does the player perform the same action in the same way with the same result every time?

Score 0 if repeated activities are identical every time — the player goes through the same motions with no meaningful variation. Score 2 if some variation exists but the core loop still feels repetitive after moderate play. Score 4 if repeated activities feel consistently fresh — variation in context, challenge, or approach makes each repetition distinct enough that the player doesn't experience rote grinding.

### J10. Downtime Management (0-5) `[CONDITIONAL: game has multi-agent turns with player downtime]`

When other agents (AI factions, NPC turns, etc.) are acting, examine what the player experiences. Check:
- Does the player have something to observe, think about, or plan during other agents' turns?
- Is other-agent activity visible and informative, or does the game simply say "waiting..."?
- Can the player use downtime productively to plan their next move based on what they observe?
- Is downtime duration proportional to its informational value?

Score 0 if the player sits idle during other agents' turns with nothing to see, think about, or learn from. Score 2 if some information is available during downtime but it is not well-presented or the downtime is disproportionately long. Score 4 if downtime is productive — the player observes meaningful events, gathers information, and plans their next action, making the wait feel like an active part of play rather than an interruption.

### J11. Equilibrium Disruption (0-5)

Check whether the game prevents itself from settling into a stable, solved state. Check:
- Does the game have internal disruption mechanisms — character events, system crises, environmental shifts, factional dynamics — that periodically upset established patterns?
- Can the player reach a comfortable equilibrium and stay there indefinitely, or do disruptions force continuous adaptation?
- Do disruptions arise from within the simulation (system interactions, autonomous agents, resource dynamics) rather than from arbitrary external events?

Score 0 if the game reaches a stable equilibrium that the player can maintain indefinitely — once optimized, nothing changes. Score 2 if some disruptions occur but they are infrequent, easily handled, or feel arbitrary rather than systemic. Score 4 if the game consistently generates internal disruptions that prevent equilibrium — the player's established patterns are regularly challenged by events arising from the simulation itself, forcing continuous adaptation and re-optimization.
