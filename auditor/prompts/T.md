# Category T: Goal Structure

You are evaluating how well the game communicates what the player should strive for and whether its goal systems create meaningful direction and tension. Focus on whether goals are legible, layered, achievable through player action, and in productive conflict with each other.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### T1. Goal Clarity (0-5)

From the player's perspective at any given point in the game, check:
- Does the player know what they are trying to accomplish? Is there a legible objective — stated explicitly or clearly implied by the game's structure?
- Can the player distinguish between "I'm making progress" and "I'm wasting my time"?
- If the game is open-ended, does it provide enough structure for the player to formulate their own goals, or does it leave them directionless?

Score 0 if the player has no idea what they should be doing — no objectives are communicated, no progress indicators exist, and the game provides no structural hints about direction. Score 2 if the player has a general sense of purpose but specific objectives are vague, or progress toward them is difficult to assess. Score 4 if the player always knows what they are working toward, can assess their progress, and understands what actions will advance their goals.

### T2. Goal Hierarchy (0-5)

Examine the game's objectives at different timescales. Check:
- Are there immediate goals (what should I do this turn)?
- Are there short-term goals (what am I building toward over the next several turns)?
- Are there long-term goals (what is my overarching strategic objective)?
- Do the layers connect — do immediate actions visibly contribute to short-term goals, which in turn serve long-term objectives?

Score 0 if goals exist at only one timescale — either only immediate (no larger purpose) or only long-term (no actionable steps). Score 2 if goals exist at two timescales but the connection between them is weak — the player can see both a micro and macro objective but the path from one to the other is unclear. Score 4 if goals exist at three or more timescales with clear causal connections — every turn-level action is understood as contributing to a short-term milestone that advances a long-term objective.

### T3. Goal Achievability (0-5)

For each goal the game presents, check:
- Can the player achieve it through their own decisions and actions?
- Does achieving the goal depend on luck, AI cooperation, or factors outside the player's control?
- Is the path from "here" to "goal achieved" composed of actions the player can actually take?

Score 0 if goals are purely aspirational — the player can see what they want but has no reliable means to get there, with outcomes determined by randomness or AI behavior. Score 2 if goals are achievable but the player's contribution is diluted by significant uncontrollable factors — skill matters but luck matters roughly as much. Score 4 if goal achievement is primarily a function of player skill and decision-making — the player can trace a clear line from their actions to the outcome, with randomness adding texture but not determining results.

### T4. Competing Objectives (0-5)

Identify all goals the game presents to the player simultaneously. Check:
- Do any of these goals conflict with each other — does pursuing one make another harder?
- Must the player make trade-offs between goals, or can they pursue everything at once?
- Does the tension between competing objectives create interesting decisions?

Score 0 if there is a single objective with no tension — the player always knows exactly what to optimize and never faces a trade-off between goals. Score 2 if competing objectives exist but the trade-offs are shallow — one goal is clearly more important and the others can be safely deprioritized. Score 4 if multiple objectives genuinely compete for the player's limited resources (time, actions, materials) and the correct prioritization depends on the current situation — there is no fixed hierarchy.

### T5. Open-Ended Goal Support (0-5)

For games with sandbox, open-ended, or freeform elements, check:
- Does the game provide systems, metrics, or feedback loops that support self-directed goal-setting?
- Can the player set a personal objective (e.g., "build the largest city," "dominate through trade") and find that the game's systems meaningfully respond to and track progress toward that goal?
- Or is the sandbox an empty field — freedom without structure, where the player can do anything but nothing feels purposeful?

Score 0 if the game offers open-ended play but provides no systems to support self-directed goals — the player has freedom but no tools to make that freedom meaningful. Score 2 if some self-directed goals are supported but the game's systems only partially track or respond to them — the player can set goals but the game doesn't fully acknowledge them. Score 4 if the game actively supports self-directed goals with systems that track progress, provide feedback, and make player-defined objectives feel as real and consequential as designer-defined ones.
