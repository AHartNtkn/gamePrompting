# Category D: Decision Architecture

You are evaluating whether the game presents the player with meaningful, well-constructed choices. Focus on the quality, frequency, structure, and consequences of the decisions the player faces.

## Criteria and Evaluation Instructions

### D1. Decision-Making Centrality (0-5)

Observe what the player actually does during gameplay, turn by turn. Check:
- Is the core activity making decisions — weighing options, choosing between alternatives, committing to a course of action?
- Or is the player mostly watching events unfold, reacting to reflex prompts, performing rote execution, or grinding repetitive tasks?
- What percentage of the player's time is spent in genuine decision-making versus non-decisional activity?

Score 0 if the player rarely makes decisions — gameplay consists primarily of watching, grinding, or executing predetermined sequences. Score 2 if decisions occur regularly but are interspersed with significant stretches of non-decisional activity. Score 4 if decision-making is the dominant activity — nearly every player action involves weighing options and choosing, with minimal dead time.

### D2. Decision Meaningfulness (0-5)

For each decision the player faces, trace the outcomes of each available option. Check:
- Do different choices produce distinct, consequential outcomes?
- Or are the options functionally equivalent — different labels on the same result?
- Does each choice change the game state in a way that matters for future gameplay?

Score 0 if choices are illusory — all options lead to effectively the same outcome, and no decision changes anything that matters. Score 2 if most choices produce some difference but many are trivial or the differences are minor and short-lived. Score 4 if every decision produces clearly distinct outcomes that meaningfully alter the game state and shape future gameplay.

### D3. Decision Frequency (0-5)

Track the rate at which the player encounters genuinely interesting decisions during gameplay. Check:
- How many interesting decisions does the player face per turn or per unit of play time?
- Are there long stretches where the player is not making meaningful choices?
- Is the pacing consistent, or do decisions cluster and then disappear?

Score 0 if interesting decisions are rare — the player goes many turns between genuinely meaningful choices. Score 2 if decisions occur at a moderate rate but with noticeable gaps or uneven pacing. Score 4 if the player faces interesting decisions at a consistently high rate with no significant dead stretches.

### D4. Decision Ambiguity (0-5)

Examine the decisions the player faces and determine whether they have clearly correct answers. Check:
- Can the optimal choice be determined by simple math or obvious logic?
- Or are decisions genuinely ambiguous — multiple options with non-obvious trade-offs where reasonable players might disagree?
- Are there decisions where the player must weigh incommensurable values (safety vs. speed, short-term vs. long-term)?

Score 0 if every decision has a clearly correct answer that can be determined by basic analysis — there is no genuine dilemma. Score 2 if some decisions are ambiguous but others are solvable puzzles with obvious best choices. Score 4 if decisions are genuinely ambiguous — multiple options are plausibly good, trade-offs are real, and the player must exercise judgment rather than calculation.

### D5. Trade-off Clarity (0-5)

For each decision, check whether the player can understand what they gain and what they sacrifice. Check:
- Are the costs and benefits of each option legible before committing?
- Can the player articulate what they are giving up when they choose one option over another?
- Or are trade-offs hidden, requiring the player to guess at consequences?

Score 0 if trade-offs are invisible — the player cannot determine what any option costs or provides before choosing. Score 2 if some trade-offs are clear but others are opaque or misleadingly presented. Score 4 if the player can clearly see what each option offers and what it costs, enabling informed decision-making without requiring complete certainty about outcomes.

### D6. Triangularity (0-5)

Identify choices that involve risk/reward trade-offs. Check:
- Are there decisions where a safe option offers a modest payoff and a risky option offers a larger payoff?
- Does the risk create genuine tension — the player wants the reward but fears the downside?
- Or are options either all-safe or all-risky, with no spectrum of risk/reward to navigate?

Score 0 if no risk/reward trade-offs exist — options are either equivalent in risk or there is no meaningful downside to any choice. Score 2 if some triangular choices exist but they are infrequent or the risk/reward ratios are poorly calibrated (risk too low or too high to create tension). Score 4 if risk/reward trade-offs are a regular feature of decision-making, with well-calibrated tensions where the risky option is genuinely tempting and the safe option is genuinely safe.

### D7. Expected Value Reasoning (0-5)

Check whether the player has enough information to reason probabilistically about their choices. Check:
- Can the player estimate the likelihood of different outcomes for a given action?
- Is enough information available (probabilities, state data, historical patterns) for the player to make an informed gamble?
- Or must the player act blindly, with no basis for estimating outcomes?

Score 0 if the player has no basis for reasoning about outcome probabilities — choices are blind gambles with no information to inform them. Score 2 if some information is available but key probabilities or factors are hidden, making reasoning partially possible. Score 4 if the player can reason about expected values with sufficient information — they know enough to make informed bets, even if uncertainty remains about exact outcomes.

### D8. Strategic Depth (0-5)

Examine the space of long-term approaches the player can pursue. Check:
- Are there multiple viable strategies that lead to success through different paths?
- Can different players succeed with fundamentally different approaches (aggressive vs. defensive, economic vs. military, diplomatic vs. isolationist)?
- Or is there a single dominant strategy that makes all others suboptimal?

Score 0 if a single dominant strategy exists — one approach is clearly superior and all others are traps. Score 2 if a few strategies are viable but the space is narrow, with most approaches being clearly inferior. Score 4 if multiple fundamentally different strategies are viable and competitive, allowing diverse playstyles to succeed.

### D9. Tactical Granularity (0-5)

Examine the moment-to-moment choices within a single turn or action. Check:
- Do individual tactical decisions matter, or do they blur together into insignificance?
- Can a single well-chosen action change the course of a battle, negotiation, or scenario?
- Or does the game only care about big-picture strategy, with individual turns being interchangeable?

Score 0 if individual tactical choices are meaningless — turns blur together and no single decision matters on its own. Score 2 if some tactical decisions carry weight but many turns are rote or interchangeable. Score 4 if individual tactical decisions are significant — the player can point to specific moments where a well-chosen action meaningfully changed the outcome.

### D10. Choice Reversibility (0-5)

Categorize the game's choices by whether they can be undone, and evaluate the appropriateness of that classification. Check:
- Are permanent choices reserved for high-stakes decisions where irreversibility creates weight and drama?
- Are reversible choices available where experimentation serves gameplay?
- Or is the mapping wrong — trivial choices are permanently locked in while major decisions can be freely undone?

Score 0 if reversibility is assigned arbitrarily — trivial choices are permanent while consequential ones are freely reversible, or everything is reversible (eliminating stakes), or everything is permanent (punishing experimentation). Score 2 if some choices have appropriate reversibility but others are misassigned. Score 4 if reversibility is deliberately distributed — permanent choices create meaningful stakes, reversible choices encourage healthy experimentation, and the mapping between importance and permanence is intentional.

### D11. Choice Awareness (0-5)

Play through the game and identify every point where a player decision shapes outcomes. Check:
- Does the player know they are making a choice at each of these points?
- Are decision moments clearly signaled — does the game present options explicitly?
- Or do consequential choices happen invisibly, where the player unknowingly commits to a path without realizing it?

Score 0 if consequential choices are invisible — the player unknowingly makes binding decisions without any indication that a choice point has occurred. Score 2 if most major choices are signaled but some significant decisions happen without the player's awareness. Score 4 if every decision point is clearly signaled and the player always knows when they are making a choice that will shape outcomes.

### D12. Consequence Visibility (0-5)

After the player makes a choice, check whether the results are visible and traceable. Check:
- Can the player observe what happened as a result of their decision?
- Is the causal chain from decision to outcome clear — can the player connect the effect back to the cause?
- Or do consequences occur silently, with the player unable to tell what their choices produced?

Score 0 if consequences are invisible — the player cannot determine what happened as a result of their decisions. Score 2 if some consequences are visible but the causal chain is often unclear or major effects go unreported. Score 4 if the player can clearly observe the results of every decision and trace the causal chain from choice to outcome.

### D13. Consequence Delay (0-5)

Measure the time between when a choice is made and when its consequences manifest. Check:
- Do tactical decisions produce immediate feedback, allowing the player to learn and adjust?
- Do strategic decisions produce delayed consequences that reward long-term planning?
- Or is timing mismatched — tactical choices taking forever to resolve while strategic choices have instant payoffs?

Score 0 if consequence timing is systematically wrong — tactical choices have delayed feedback (preventing learning) or strategic choices resolve immediately (eliminating planning). Score 2 if timing is mostly appropriate but some decisions have mismatched consequence delays. Score 4 if consequence timing is deliberately calibrated — immediate feedback for tactical decisions, delayed consequences for strategic ones, with the delay serving the type of thinking each decision demands.

### D14. Consequence Integration (0-5)

Trace the outcomes of player actions and check whether they feed back into the larger game context. Check:
- Do the consequences of decisions ripple outward, affecting other systems and future situations?
- Or are consequences locally resolved and forgotten — affecting only the immediate situation and nothing beyond?
- Does winning a battle affect the war? Does a trade deal affect the economy? Does a dialogue choice affect a relationship?

Score 0 if consequences are locally resolved and forgotten — each situation is self-contained with no connection to the broader game. Score 2 if some consequences integrate into the larger context but many are isolated. Score 4 if the outcomes of player decisions routinely feed into the larger game context, affecting multiple systems and shaping future situations.

### D15. Multi-Dimensional Strategy (0-5) `[CONDITIONAL: game has multiple orthogonal management domains]`

Identify the distinct strategic domains the player must manage simultaneously. Check:
- Are there multiple orthogonal concerns that compete for the player's attention and resources (e.g., military, economic, diplomatic, internal stability)?
- Do these domains interact — does a decision in one domain create consequences in another?
- Must the player genuinely balance these concerns, or can they safely ignore some domains?

Score 0 if the game presents only a single strategic dimension — all decisions reduce to one concern. Score 2 if multiple domains exist but the player can safely focus on one while neglecting others. Score 4 if the player must actively balance multiple orthogonal strategic domains, with each demanding attention and decisions in one creating trade-offs in others.

### D16. Plan Fragility (0-5)

Set a plan and attempt to execute it over multiple turns. Check:
- Can the plan be carried out without disruption, or do unforeseen events force adaptation?
- Does the game generate situations that invalidate prior plans, requiring the player to re-evaluate?
- Or can the player set a strategy and execute it mechanically without any need to adjust?

Score 0 if plans are never disrupted — the player can set a course and execute it mechanically with no surprises or adaptation required. Score 2 if plans are occasionally disrupted but the player can mostly stick to their initial approach. Score 4 if plans are regularly disrupted by unforeseen but logical events, requiring the player to constantly adapt, re-evaluate, and adjust their strategy.

### D17. Consequence Irreversibility Spectrum (0-5)

Survey all the decisions in the game and categorize each by how permanent its consequences are. Check:
- Is there a graduated range of permanence — some decisions easily reversible, some costly to reverse, some truly permanent?
- Is this distribution intentional and well-calibrated, with permanence matching the weight of the decision?
- Or does the game have a flat permanence profile — all decisions equally reversible or all equally permanent?

Score 0 if the game has a flat permanence profile — all decisions are equally reversible (no stakes) or equally permanent (no experimentation), with no graduation. Score 2 if some variation in permanence exists but it is not well-distributed or well-matched to decision weight. Score 4 if the game provides a deliberate spectrum from easily reversible decisions (enabling experimentation) through costly-to-reverse decisions (creating stakes) to fully permanent decisions (creating weight), with the permanence level matching the significance of each decision.
