# Game Design Evaluation Criteria — Comprehensive Research

Research compiled from academic frameworks, professional practice, game design theory, and community evaluation standards. Each section extracts specific evaluable dimensions from its source.

---

## 1. MDA Framework (Hunicke, LeBlanc, Zubek, 2004)

Source: "MDA: A Formal Approach to Game Design and Game Research" — GDC / AAAI Workshop

The MDA framework decomposes games into three layers. Evaluation criteria derive from asking whether each layer is well-designed and whether the layers connect coherently.

### Mechanics-Level Criteria
- **Rule clarity**: Are the base rules unambiguous and learnable?
- **Action space adequacy**: Does the set of player actions cover the space the game intends to simulate?
- **Mechanic orthogonality**: Do distinct mechanics serve distinct purposes, or are they redundant?
- **Data transparency**: Can the player observe or infer the relevant algorithmic state?

### Dynamics-Level Criteria
- **Emergent behavior**: Do mechanics interact at runtime to produce non-scripted outcomes?
- **Dynamic variety**: Do different player inputs produce observably different runtime behaviors?
- **Feedback loop health**: Are positive/negative feedback loops present and intentional? Do they stabilize or destabilize the game appropriately?
- **Exploit resistance**: Does the dynamics layer resist degenerate strategies?

### Aesthetics-Level Criteria (The Eight Aesthetics)
Each aesthetic is a distinct emotional response the game can target. Evaluable question: does the game successfully evoke it?

1. **Sensation** — Does the game deliver sensory pleasure (visual, auditory, tactile)?
2. **Fantasy** — Does the game enable make-believe / inhabiting an alternate identity?
3. **Narrative** — Does the game produce dramatic arcs (authored or emergent)?
4. **Challenge** — Does the game present obstacles that test the player's capabilities?
5. **Fellowship** — Does the game create social bonding or cooperative dynamics?
6. **Discovery** — Does the game reward exploration and uncharted-territory seeking?
7. **Expression** — Does the game allow self-expression and identity crafting?
8. **Submission** — Does the game serve as an effective pastime / zone-out activity?

### Cross-Layer Criteria
- **Mechanic-aesthetic alignment**: Do the mechanics actually produce the intended aesthetic? (e.g., if discovery is the goal, is there enough hidden content?)
- **Layer coherence**: Does the dynamics layer faithfully translate mechanics into aesthetics, or are there disconnects?

## 2. Schell's Lenses (Jesse Schell, "The Art of Game Design", 3rd ed.)

Source: 100+ lenses, each framed as a set of diagnostic questions. Relevant lenses extracted and stated as evaluable criteria.

### Experience & Emotion
- **Lens #1 — Emotion**: What emotions does the game evoke? Are they the intended ones?
- **Lens #2 — Essential Experience**: What experience is the game trying to create, and does every element serve that experience?
- **Lens #4 — Surprise**: Does the game produce genuine surprise? Is surprise well-paced?
- **Lens #5 — Fun**: What parts are fun and why? What parts are not fun and why?
- **Lens #6 — Curiosity**: Does the game raise questions the player wants answered?
- **Lens #12 — Resonance**: Does the game feel like it is about something that matters?

### Structure & Mechanics
- **Lens #7 — Endogenous Value**: Do game objects have value that is meaningful within the game's internal logic?
- **Lens #8 — Problem Solving**: What problems does the game ask the player to solve? Are they interesting?
- **Lens #9 — Elemental Tetrad**: Are mechanics, story, aesthetics, and technology balanced and mutually reinforcing?
- **Lens #11 — Unification**: Does every element serve a single unifying theme or vision?
- **Lens #26 — Functional Space**: Does the game's spatial design serve gameplay?
- **Lens #32 — Meaningful Choices**: Are choices presented to the player truly meaningful (distinct outcomes, informed, non-reversible)?
- **Lens #33 — Triangularity**: Do choices involve risk/reward trade-offs that create tension?

### Player & Skill
- **Lens #19 — The Player**: Does the game account for who the player is and what they want?
- **Lens #21 — Flow**: Does difficulty scale with player skill to maintain the flow channel?
- **Lens #27 — Skill**: What skills does the game require? Is the required skill set appropriate and satisfying?
- **Lens #29 — Chance**: Is randomness used to create interesting situations, not to negate player agency?
- **Lens #30 — Fairness**: Does the game feel fair? Does the player believe their outcomes are deserved?
- **Lens #31 — Challenge**: Is the game appropriately challenging — neither trivially easy nor impossibly hard?

### Balance & Economy
- **Lens #28 — Expected Value**: Can the player reason about the expected value of their choices?
- **Lens #34 — Skill vs. Chance balance**: Is the ratio of skill to luck appropriate for the game's goals?
- **Lens #35 — Head vs. Hands**: Is the ratio of mental to physical challenge appropriate?
- **Lens #36 — Competition vs. Cooperation**: Is the balance between competitive and cooperative elements appropriate?
- **Lens #46 — Economy**: Is the game's internal economy balanced? Do currencies/resources flow properly?

### Narrative & World
- **Lens #61 — Interest Curve**: Does engagement rise and fall in a satisfying rhythm over time?
- **Lens #63 — Beauty**: Is there aesthetic coherence? Does the game have a consistent visual/tonal identity?
- **Lens #70 — Story**: Does the story (if any) emerge from gameplay rather than interrupting it?
- **Lens #71 — Freedom**: Does the player feel free, or constrained by the game's structure?
- **Lens #74 — The World**: Is the game world internally consistent and believable on its own terms?

### Meta-Design
- **Lens #10 — Holographic Design**: Does every element of the game reinforce every other element?
- **Lens #20 — Pleasure**: Does the game produce multiple types of pleasure (sensory, social, intellectual)?
- **Lens #22 — Needs (Maslow)**: Does the game satisfy higher-order needs (esteem, self-actualization, belonging)?
- **Lens #24 — Novelty**: Does the game continue to present novel situations over time?
- **Lens #25 — Judgment**: Is the game's judgment of the player (scoring, feedback) perceived as fair and motivating?

## 3. Salen & Zimmerman — Rules of Play (2003)

Source: "Rules of Play: Game Design Fundamentals" — 18 game design schemas

### Core Evaluable Concepts

- **Meaningful play (discernible)**: Can the player perceive the relationship between their actions and the game's response?
- **Meaningful play (integrated)**: Do the outcomes of player actions feed into the larger context of the game?
- **Constitutive rules**: Are the underlying formal rules (math, algorithms) well-designed?
- **Operational rules**: Are the rules-as-played intuitive and learnable?
- **Implicit rules**: Are the unwritten social/behavioral expectations of play clear?
- **Information structure**: Is the game's information (perfect, imperfect, complete, incomplete) appropriate for its design goals?
- **System openness vs. closure**: Does the game have clear boundaries, or does it bleed into outside systems?
- **Emergence**: Do simple rules produce complex behavior?
- **Uncertainty**: Is the game's outcome genuinely uncertain until the end?
- **Magic circle integrity**: Does the game successfully create a bounded, internally-meaningful space?
- **Lusory attitude**: Does the game reward players who adopt the "spirit of play" rather than exploit the rules?
- **Conflict structure**: Is the game's core conflict well-defined and engaging?
- **Outcome resolution**: Is the way the game determines winners/losers satisfying?
- **Game as cultural rhetoric**: Does the game say something through its systems (whether or not it intends to)?

## 4. Costikyan — "I Have No Words & I Must Design" (1994/2002)

Source: Foundational game design vocabulary paper

### Evaluable Dimensions

- **Decision-making centrality**: Is the core activity of the game making decisions (vs. reflex, luck, or passive consumption)?
- **Goal structure**: Does the game provide clear goals that motivate play?
- **Opposition/struggle**: Is there meaningful resistance to the player's objectives?
- **Resource management**: Does the player manage scarce resources toward their goals?
- **Information management**: Does the game require the player to gather, process, and act on information?
- **Game state mutability**: Does the game state change in response to player actions (vs. being static like a puzzle)?
- **Token identification**: Can the player identify with their game token / avatar / position?
- **Interaction richness**: How many distinct ways can the player interact with the game system?

## 5. Costikyan — "Uncertainty in Games" (2013)

Source: MIT Press — Taxonomy of 11 types of uncertainty

Each type is an evaluable dimension: does the game use this type of uncertainty, and is it well-calibrated?

1. **Performative uncertainty** — Will I be able to physically/mentally execute this action?
2. **Solver's uncertainty** — Can I find the solution to this problem?
3. **Player unpredictability** — What will other agents (human or AI) do?
4. **Randomness** — What will chance produce?
5. **Analytic complexity** — Can I reason through this complex decision tree?
6. **Hidden information** — What is being deliberately withheld from me?
7. **Narrative anticipation** — What is going to happen next in the story?
8. **Development anticipation** — What new content/features will appear?
9. **Schedule uncertainty** — When will I next play?
10. **Perception uncertainty** — Am I perceiving the game state correctly?
11. **Semiotic uncertainty** — What does my play of this game mean?

### Meta-Criteria
- **Uncertainty calibration**: Is the total uncertainty sufficient to maintain interest but not so high as to feel arbitrary?
- **Uncertainty source diversity**: Does the game draw from multiple uncertainty types, or is it one-dimensional?

## 6. Koster — "A Theory of Fun" (2004)

Source: Raph Koster's model of fun as pattern-learning

### Evaluable Dimensions

- **Pattern richness**: Does the game contain learnable patterns at multiple levels of complexity?
- **Learning curve continuity**: Is there always something new to learn, or does learning plateau?
- **Mastery depth**: How deep can mastery go? Is there always a higher level of play to achieve?
- **Grokking resistance**: How long before the player fully "groks" (internalizes) all patterns?
- **Boredom onset**: At what point does the player run out of new patterns to learn? Is that point satisfyingly far into the experience?
- **Pattern visibility**: Can the player perceive the patterns they are learning, or are they opaque?
- **Skill-expression space**: Once patterns are learned, can the player express mastery in varied ways?
- **Degenerate pattern detection**: Are there patterns that, once learned, trivialize the game?

## 7. Burgun — "Game Design Theory" (2012) / "Clockwork Game Design" (2015)

Source: Keith Burgun's formal interactive-system hierarchy and clockwork methodology

### Hierarchy Evaluation (What type of system is this?)
- **Toy**: Does it have prescribed goals? (If no, it is a toy — evaluate as a toy)
- **Puzzle**: Is the challenge solvable with a known optimal solution? (If yes, it is a puzzle)
- **Contest**: Is the primary uncertainty performative? (If yes, it is a contest)
- **Game**: Does it require decision-making under obfuscated state? (If yes, it is a game proper)

### Clockwork Criteria
- **Core clarity**: Does the game have a single, clear core mechanic that everything else serves?
- **Core support**: Does every additional mechanic support the core, or does it pull in a different direction?
- **Noise elimination**: Have unnecessary elements been removed? Does every rule earn its place?
- **Decision quality**: Are decisions in the game genuinely ambiguous (no clearly correct answer)?
- **State obfuscation**: Is the game state sufficiently hidden to require reasoning rather than calculation?
- **Arc structure**: Does the game have a well-defined arc from opening through midgame to resolution?

## 8. Csikszentmihalyi Flow Theory (Applied to Games)

Source: Csikszentmihalyi's flow theory, adapted by Chen (2006), Sweetser & Wyeth (2005)

### Flow Channel Criteria
- **Skill-challenge balance**: Is the challenge calibrated to the player's current skill level?
- **Anxiety avoidance**: Does the game avoid overwhelming the player with excessive difficulty?
- **Boredom avoidance**: Does the game avoid under-stimulating the player with trivial tasks?
- **Apathy avoidance**: Does the game avoid states where both skill demand and challenge are low?
- **Dynamic difficulty**: Does the difficulty adapt (explicitly or through player-chosen paths) as skill grows?

### Flow State Indicators
- **Clear goals at all times**: Does the player always know what they should be doing?
- **Immediate feedback**: Does the player receive instant feedback on every action?
- **Sense of control**: Does the player feel in control of their actions and their consequences?
- **Loss of self-consciousness**: Can the player become fully absorbed?
- **Time distortion**: Does the player lose track of time while playing?
- **Autotelic experience**: Is the activity intrinsically rewarding (not just instrumentally)?

## 9. GameFlow Model (Sweetser & Wyeth, 2005)

Source: "GameFlow: A Model for Evaluating Player Enjoyment in Games" — ACM Computers in Entertainment

Eight evaluable dimensions, each with sub-criteria:

### 9.1 Concentration
- Does the game demand focus without overwhelming the player?
- Does the game minimize non-essential stimuli?
- Is the player's workload appropriate for the task?

### 9.2 Challenge
- Are challenges matched to the player's skill level?
- Is there a variety of challenge types?
- Does the difficulty increase at an appropriate rate?

### 9.3 Player Skills
- Does the game develop player skills at a consistent pace?
- Are skills developed through play (not just tutorials)?
- Does the game reward skilled play?

### 9.4 Control
- Does the player feel a sense of control over their actions?
- Are controls intuitive and responsive?
- Can the player recover from errors?

### 9.5 Clear Goals
- Are overarching goals presented early?
- Are short-term goals provided throughout gameplay?
- Are goals appropriate and achievable?

### 9.6 Feedback
- Does the player receive immediate feedback on actions?
- Does the player receive feedback on progress toward goals?
- Is the player's current status always visible?

### 9.7 Immersion
- Does the player become less aware of surroundings while playing?
- Does the player become less self-aware while playing?
- Does the player experience an altered sense of time?

### 9.8 Social Interaction
- Does the game support social interaction between players?
- Does the game create competition between players?
- Does the game support cooperation between players?

## 10. Player Experience of Need Satisfaction (PENS)

Source: Ryan, Rigby & Przybylski — based on Self-Determination Theory

Five evaluable dimensions:

1. **Competence** — Does the player feel effective and capable within the game?
2. **Autonomy** — Does the player feel that their choices are self-directed and meaningful?
3. **Relatedness** — Does the player feel connected to other characters or players?
4. **Presence/Immersion** — Does the player feel physically or emotionally present in the game world?
5. **Intuitive controls** — Does the interface feel natural and unobtrusive?

## 11. Game Experience Questionnaire (GEQ)

Source: IJsselsteijn, de Kort, Poels — Eindhoven University of Technology

Seven evaluable dimensions (core module):

1. **Competence** — Did the player feel skilled and capable?
2. **Immersion** — Did the player feel absorbed in the game world?
3. **Flow** — Did the player lose track of time and effort?
4. **Positive affect** — Did the player experience positive emotions?
5. **Negative affect** — Did the player experience negative emotions?
6. **Tension/Annoyance** — Did the player feel frustrated or annoyed?
7. **Challenge** — Did the player feel appropriately challenged?

## 12. Game Feel / Juice (Steve Swink, 2009)

Source: "Game Feel: A Game Designer's Guide to Virtual Sensation"

### Core Game-Feel Dimensions

- **Input responsiveness**: Is there a tight coupling between input and on-screen response?
- **Control precision**: Can the player make the game token do exactly what they intend?
- **Spatial simulation fidelity**: Does the simulated space behave in ways that feel physically plausible?
- **Polish/Juice**: Do actions produce satisfying ancillary feedback (screen shake, particles, sound, animation)?
- **Sensory feedback layering**: Are visual, auditory, and haptic channels all contributing to the feel?
- **Metaphor coherence**: Does the virtual sensation match the real-world metaphor? (e.g., a "heavy" character should feel heavy)
- **Skill ceiling visibility**: Can the player perceive that there is a higher level of control mastery to achieve?

### Applicability to CLI Games
For text-based / ASCII games, "game feel" translates to:
- **Response immediacy**: Does the game respond instantly to input?
- **Feedback density**: Does action feedback contain rich, specific information?
- **State change visibility**: Are the consequences of actions immediately visible in the display?
- **Rhythm and cadence**: Does the interaction loop have a satisfying tempo?

## 13. Heuristic Evaluation for Games (Pinelle, Wong & Stach, 2008)

Source: CHI 2008 — "Heuristic Evaluation for Games: Usability Principles for Video Game Design"

Ten usability heuristics derived from analysis of 108 game reviews:

1. **Consistent controls**: Does the game respond consistently to the same input?
2. **Customizable controls**: Can the player remap or adjust controls?
3. **Predictable game behavior**: Does the game behave in ways the player can anticipate?
4. **Appropriate visual/audio feedback**: Are interactions communicated through sensory channels?
5. **Lack of burden on player memory**: Does the game avoid requiring the player to memorize arbitrary information?
6. **Navigable menus/interfaces**: Can the player navigate the interface efficiently?
7. **Skip non-playable content**: Can the player skip cutscenes, intros, or other non-interactive content?
8. **Adjustable difficulty**: Can the player adjust the game's difficulty level?
9. **Recovery from errors**: Can the player recover from mistakes without catastrophic consequences?
10. **Clear instructions**: Are controls and objectives explained adequately?

## 14. PLAY Heuristics (Desurvire & Wiberg, 2009)

Source: "Game Usability Heuristics (PLAY) for Evaluating and Designing Better Games"

Heuristics organized into four categories:

### Game Play
- **Enduring play**: Is the game fun with no repetitive or boring tasks?
- **Challenge/strategy/pace balance**: Are these three elements in harmony?
- **Varied challenge**: Does the game provide a variety of challenge types?
- **Player control**: Does the player feel in control of their experience?
- **Perceived fairness**: Does the player perceive the game as fair?

### Game Usability
- **Error handling**: Can the player recognize and recover from errors?
- **Consistent interface**: Is the interface consistently designed throughout?
- **Feedback adequacy**: Does the player receive sufficient feedback?
- **Status visibility**: Is the player's status and progress visible?
- **Minimal memory burden**: Does the game avoid unnecessary memory demands?
- **Learnability**: Can the player learn through play rather than instruction?

### Game Mechanics
- **Consistent world**: Does the game world behave consistently?
- **Appropriate AI**: Does the AI behave believably and fairly?
- **Balanced difficulty**: Is difficulty appropriately balanced?

### Game Story
- **Story integration**: Is the story integrated with gameplay?
- **Story pacing**: Does the story unfold at an appropriate pace?
- **Character development**: Do characters develop meaningfully?

## 16. Zileas' Anti-Fun Patterns (Tom Cadwell / Riot Games)

Source: League of Legends design philosophy — patterns that indicate design failure

Each anti-pattern is an evaluable negative criterion (its presence indicates a flaw):

1. **Burden of Knowledge** — Does a mechanic require the victim to have read external documentation to understand what happened to them?
2. **Power Without Gameplay** — Does a mechanic provide benefit without the player noticing or making decisions about it?
3. **Anti-Fun Exceeds Fun** — Does a mechanic's negative impact on opponents exceed its positive experience for the user?
4. **False Choice / Ineffective Choice** — Does a choice appear meaningful but actually have no real consequence or one clearly dominant option?
5. **Unclear Optimization** — Is it unclear to the player how to improve their performance?
6. **Anti-Combo** — Do the player's abilities/items undermine each other?
7. **Use Pattern Mismatch** — Does an ability require the player to act against the logic of their role/class?

## 17. Game Design Anti-Patterns & Dark Patterns

### Degenerate Strategies (General)
- **Dominant strategy presence** — Is there one strategy that beats all others regardless of context?
- **Degenerate strategy vulnerability** — Can a player exploit a rules weakness to guarantee victory without engaging with intended gameplay?
- **Illusion of choice** — Do presented options converge to the same outcome regardless of selection?
- **Grinding viability** — Can the player bypass challenge by spending time on trivial repeated actions?

### Dark Patterns (Zagal, Bjork & Lewis, 2013)
Three categories of manipulative design:

**Temporal dark patterns:**
- Does the game require real-time waiting to progress?
- Does the game punish the player for not returning regularly?
- Does the game make the player repeat content unnecessarily?

**Monetary dark patterns:**
- Does the game create artificial frustration to incentivize spending?
- Does the game obscure the real-money cost of in-game items?
- Does the game create pay-to-win advantages?

**Social capital dark patterns:**
- Does the game exploit social pressure (guilting friends, competitive spending)?
- Does the game expose player spending to create status anxiety?

### Cheese Detection (General)
- **Exploit-to-skill ratio** — Can the player "cheese" encounters rather than engage with intended mechanics?
- **AI manipulation** — Can the player trivially manipulate AI behavior to avoid challenge?
- **Sequence breaking** — Can the player bypass intended progression through unintended mechanics?

## 18. Sid Meier's Design Principles

Source: GDC talks, "Sid Meier's Memoir!"

### Evaluable Criteria

- **Interesting decisions per unit time** — How frequently does the player face decisions where multiple options are viable and the outcome matters?
- **Player-as-star** — Does the game make the player feel like the protagonist of their experience?
- **2:1 reward ratio** — Does the game reward success roughly twice as much as it punishes failure?
- **Clear consequences** — Can the player understand the downstream effects of their decisions?
- **One more turn compulsion** — Does the game create forward-looking anticipation that makes the player want to continue?
- **Prototype-first** — (Development criterion) Was the fun found through iteration rather than designed top-down?

## 19. Bartle Player Type Taxonomy

Source: Richard Bartle (1996) — four player motivations

Evaluable as "does the game serve this player type?"

1. **Achiever satisfaction** — Does the game provide concrete measurements of success (scores, levels, completion)?
2. **Explorer satisfaction** — Does the game reward exploration, discovery, and understanding of systems?
3. **Socializer satisfaction** — Does the game enable meaningful social interaction?
4. **Killer satisfaction** — Does the game allow competitive dominance and skill expression against others?

For single-player/CLI games, the most relevant types are Achiever and Explorer. Evaluable question: does the game provide both concrete achievement metrics AND discovery/exploration rewards?

## 20. Berlin Interpretation — Roguelike Design (2008)

Source: International Roguelike Development Conference

### High-Value Factors (9 criteria)
1. **Random environment generation** — Is the game world procedurally generated to increase replayability?
2. **Permadeath** — Does death end the run permanently, creating stakes?
3. **Turn-based** — Does the game process one player action at a time, allowing deliberation?
4. **Grid-based** — Is movement/positioning on a discrete grid?
5. **Non-modal** — Can the player access all actions from a single mode (no separate screens for combat vs. exploration)?
6. **Complexity** — Does the game have enough systems to enable multiple solutions to problems?
7. **Resource management** — Must the player manage finite resources (food, potions, equipment durability)?
8. **Hack-and-slash** — Is combat with numerous enemies a central activity?
9. **Exploration and discovery** — Must the player explore unknown territory and discover unidentified items?

### Low-Value Factors (6 criteria)
1. **Single player character** — Does the player control one character at a time?
2. **Monsters as similar to players** — Do enemies follow the same rules as the player?
3. **Tactical challenge** — Does each encounter require tactical thinking (not just stat comparison)?
4. **ASCII display** — Is the game represented with text/ASCII graphics?
5. **Dungeons** — Does the game take place in dungeon-like environments (rooms and corridors)?
6. **Numbers visible** — Are the underlying numerical values visible to the player?

## 21. Harris — Eight Rules of Roguelike Design

Source: John Harris, Gamasutra/Game Developer analysis

1. **No Beheading** — Is the player protected from instant-kill outcomes during reasonable play?
2. **No Cyanide** — Are unidentified items never immediately fatal?
3. **Item Masquerade** — Do identification systems resist spoiler/wiki-based trivialization?
4. **Situational ID Advantage** — Does the game reward contextual item identification over rote memorization?
5. **Item Enhancement** — Can items be improved through gameplay actions?
6. **Two-Sided Coin** — Do powerful items/abilities carry meaningful drawbacks?
7. **Reducing Grind** — Does the game disincentivize grinding through resource depletion or time pressure?
8. **Race You Can't Win** — Does monster difficulty scale slightly faster than player power, forcing reliance on tactics and items?

## 22. Simulation & Strategy Game Criteria

Source: Synthesized from 4X, grand strategy, and simulation design literature

### Depth vs. Complexity
- **Depth-to-complexity ratio** — How many meaningful decisions emerge per unit of rule complexity?
- **Comprehension complexity** — How hard is the game to understand?
- **Tracking complexity** — How hard is the game state to monitor during play?
- **Decision complexity** — How hard is it to find the best move? (This is the desirable form)

### 4X-Specific Criteria (Explore, Expand, Exploit, Exterminate)
- **Explore phase richness** — Is early-game exploration interesting and varied?
- **Expand decision space** — Are expansion choices meaningful (where, when, how)?
- **Exploit system depth** — Does resource exploitation involve genuine optimization problems?
- **Exterminate alternatives** — Are non-military victory paths viable and interesting?
- **Phase balance** — Are all four X's given appropriate weight, or does one dominate?
- **Snowball resistance** — Does the game prevent early advantages from becoming insurmountable?

### Grand Strategy Criteria
- **Multi-dimensional strategy** — Must the player simultaneously manage military, economic, diplomatic, and internal systems?
- **Consequence chains** — Do decisions create long chains of downstream consequences?
- **Emergent narrative generation** — Do interacting systems produce story-like sequences of events?
- **Asymmetric starting conditions** — Do different starting positions create genuinely different strategic puzzles?
- **Continuous decision rhythm** — Does the game maintain a steady stream of decisions (not long idle periods)?
- **Plan fragility** — Can plans be disrupted by unforeseen events, requiring adaptation?

### Economy Design Criteria
- **Faucet-sink balance** — Is the rate of resource creation balanced by resource consumption?
- **Inflation resistance** — Does the economy avoid runaway resource accumulation that trivializes scarcity?
- **Pinch point design** — Are there moments where resources become meaningfully scarce?
- **Currency meaningful differentiation** — Do different resources serve different purposes (not fungible)?
- **Trade-off clarity** — Can the player reason about the cost of resource allocation decisions?

## 23. Emergent Narrative & Systemic Design Criteria

Source: Synthesized from Dwarf Fortress, Rimworld, and systemic design literature

- **System interaction density** — How many mechanics interact with each other?
- **Chain reaction potential** — Can events cascade through multiple systems?
- **Narrative emergence rate** — How frequently do system interactions produce "story-worthy" events?
- **Agent personality distinctness** — Do AI entities behave in observably different ways based on their attributes?
- **Player authorship** — Does the player feel they authored the narrative through their decisions?
- **Unpredictability from determinism** — Do surprising outcomes arise from deterministic rule interactions (not random events)?
- **World coherence under stress** — Does the simulation remain internally consistent when pushed to extremes?

## 24. Board Game Review Criteria

Source: Synthesized from BoardGameGeek community frameworks

### BGG 10-Criteria Framework
1. **Mechanics quality** — Are the core mechanics well-designed and functional?
2. **Theme integration** — Is the theme reflected in the mechanics, or is it pasted on?
3. **Player interaction quality** — How do players' actions affect each other?
4. **Replayability** — Is the game worth playing many times?
5. **Scalability** — Does the game work well at different player counts?
6. **Downtime** — How long do players wait between their turns?
7. **Learning curve** — How long does it take to learn vs. how deep is mastery?
8. **Decision tension** — Are decisions tense and engaging?
9. **Component quality** — (Physical-game-specific, less relevant to CLI)
10. **Value proposition** — Is the depth-to-time-investment ratio good?

### BGG 14-Step Review Guideline
1. **Overview/summary** — What is the game about?
2. **Components** — What comes in the box?
3. **Setup** — How long/complex is setup?
4. **Gameplay description** — What does a turn look like?
5. **Theme execution** — How well does theme manifest in play?
6. **Mechanics evaluation** — How well do mechanics work?
7. **Player interaction** — How much do players affect each other?
8. **Replay value** — What drives replaying?
9. **Complexity/weight** — How heavy is the game?
10. **Player count scaling** — Does it scale well?
11. **Game length** — Is it appropriate for its depth?
12. **Fun factor** — Is it enjoyable?
13. **Target audience fit** — Who is this for?
14. **Final verdict** — Overall assessment

### Board Game Halv Framework (5 dimensions, 1-10 each)
1. **Fun** — Joy and opportunities for joy
2. **Player Interaction** — How much others' actions affect you
3. **Quality** — Rules clarity, balance, component quality
4. **Artwork & Style** — Visual coherence and direction
5. **Tone** — Emotional consistency and success of intended atmosphere

## 25. Information & Communication Design

Source: Synthesized from HCI, game design, and information design literature

- **State readability** — Can the player parse the game state quickly and accurately?
- **Information hierarchy** — Is critical information prominent and secondary information accessible but non-intrusive?
- **Progressive disclosure** — Is complexity revealed gradually as the player needs it?
- **Feedback specificity** — Does feedback explain what happened, why, and what it means?
- **Feedback timing** — Is feedback immediate enough to connect action to consequence?
- **Noise-to-signal ratio** — How much irrelevant information does the player receive relative to relevant?
- **Scan-ability** — Can the player quickly scan the display for key information?
- **Information request cost** — How many actions does it take to access needed information?
- **Mental model alignment** — Does the interface match how the player thinks about the game?
- **Error communication** — When the player makes an invalid action, does the game explain why?

### Information Asymmetry Design
- **Hidden information purpose** — Does hidden information serve a design goal (tension, discovery) or just frustrate?
- **Discoverable information** — Can hidden information be uncovered through gameplay?
- **Information advantage fairness** — Does information discovery correlate with skilled play?
- **Fog of war coherence** — Is the boundary between known and unknown logically consistent?

## 26. Player Agency & Choice Architecture

Source: Synthesized from multiple frameworks (Salen/Zimmerman, Costikyan, SDT)

- **Choice frequency** — How often does the player make meaningful choices?
- **Choice distinctness** — Do options lead to genuinely different outcomes?
- **Choice reversibility** — Are choices permanent when they should be, and reversible when that serves gameplay?
- **Choice awareness** — Does the player know they are making a choice?
- **Consequence visibility** — Can the player see the results of their choices?
- **Consequence delay** — Is the time between choice and consequence appropriate (immediate for tactical, delayed for strategic)?
- **Agency-randomness balance** — Does the player feel their choices matter more than luck?
- **Autonomy respect** — Does the game avoid forcing the player down a single path?

## 27. Reward & Motivation Design

Source: Synthesized from operant conditioning research, SDT, Koster

### Reward Schedule Evaluation
- **Variable ratio usage** — Does the game use variable reward timing to maintain engagement?
- **Reward predictability calibration** — Are some rewards predictable (motivating) and some surprising (exciting)?
- **Reward-effort correlation** — Do greater challenges yield greater rewards?
- **Reward type variety** — Does the game offer multiple types of rewards (power, knowledge, narrative, cosmetic)?

### Motivation Type Evaluation
- **Intrinsic motivation support** — Is the activity itself rewarding (mastery, discovery, expression)?
- **Extrinsic motivation appropriateness** — Are external rewards (scores, unlocks) additive rather than replacing intrinsic motivation?
- **Overjustification avoidance** — Does the game avoid making intrinsically fun activities feel like work through excessive extrinsic framing?
- **Competence feedback** — Does the game tell the player they are getting better?
- **Autonomy support** — Does the player feel their choices are self-directed?
- **Mastery curve satisfaction** — Does getting better at the game feel rewarding in itself?

## 28. CLI/Text-Game-Specific Criteria

Source: Derived from the project's focus on CLI games, Dwarf Fortress, Caves of Qud, traditional roguelikes

- **Textual imagination fuel** — Do descriptions provide enough concrete, specific detail for mental visualization?
- **ASCII art effectiveness** — If ASCII art is used, does it communicate spatial information clearly?
- **Command discoverability** — Can the player find out what actions are available without external documentation?
- **Input efficiency** — Can common actions be performed with minimal keystrokes?
- **Output density calibration** — Is text output dense enough to be informative but not so dense as to be overwhelming?
- **Contextual help** — Can the player access help for their current situation without leaving the game?
- **Turn structure clarity** — Is it always clear when the player should act and what constitutes a "turn"?
- **Save/load transparency** — Is the persistence model (permadeath, save-on-quit, free-save) clear?
- **Terminal compatibility** — Does the game work across different terminal sizes and capabilities?

- **Genre innovation** — What does this game do differently from its genre peers?

## 30. Comprehensive Anti-Pattern Checklist

Source: Synthesized from all frameworks above — things whose presence indicates design failure

### Decision Anti-Patterns
- [ ] Dominant strategy exists (one best approach regardless of context)
- [ ] False choices (options that look different but produce identical outcomes)
- [ ] Uninformed choices (player must decide without sufficient information)
- [ ] Irreversible uninformed choices (blind commitment with permanent consequences)
- [ ] Trivial choices (options where one is obviously superior)
- [ ] Excessive choices (too many options causing decision paralysis)

### Mechanical Anti-Patterns
- [ ] Dead mechanics (systems that exist but don't affect gameplay)
- [ ] Orphan mechanics (systems with no connection to other systems)
- [ ] Vestigial mechanics (systems that were once relevant but no longer serve a purpose)
- [ ] Grinding as strategy (spending time on trivial tasks is optimal)
- [ ] Snowball/runaway leader (early advantage becomes insurmountable)
- [ ] Death spiral (early disadvantage becomes irrecoverable)
- [ ] Kingmaker (eliminated player determines who wins)
- [ ] Quarterbacking (one player dictates all decisions in co-op)

### Information Anti-Patterns
- [ ] Information overload (too much data, no hierarchy)
- [ ] Information starvation (critical data is hidden or absent)
- [ ] Burden of knowledge (must read external docs to play effectively)
- [ ] Unclear optimization (player cannot tell how to improve)
- [ ] Hidden important rules (game behaves inconsistently with player's mental model)
- [ ] Misleading feedback (game communicates wrong information about state)

### Experience Anti-Patterns
- [ ] Dead time (periods where nothing meaningful happens)
- [ ] Repetition without variation (same experience repeating without novelty)
- [ ] Front-loaded depth (game gets shallower as it progresses)
- [ ] Fake difficulty (difficulty from poor controls/information, not genuine challenge)
- [ ] Punishment without learning (player fails without understanding why)
- [ ] Reward without achievement (player receives rewards for trivial actions)

### Simulation Anti-Patterns
- [ ] Rule inconsistency (same action produces different results without explanation)
- [ ] World amnesia (world doesn't remember player's actions)
- [ ] Uncanny valley of simulation (partially realistic systems that break in unrealistic ways)
- [ ] Flat world (no environmental or contextual variation)
- [ ] Clockwork predictability (every outcome is fully predictable, no emergence)
